Note on Geotrichum: A systematic PubMed search (2021–2026) found no recent peer-reviewed papers specifically isolating or characterising Geotrichum spp. as marine hydrocarbon degraders. The only documented case is the historical patent isolate Geotrichum marinum Thirumalachar (ATCC 20614), which emulsified and consumed crude petroleum but has not been revisited in recent literature. This gap is discussed in the Geotrichum section below. Papers 1–6 focus on Aspergillus.

Paper Summaries

Crude-oil degradation capabilities by microscopic fungi of deep-sea hydrothermal vents Marine Aspergillus
Salcedo et al. · 2025 · Environmental Science and Pollution Research International
Problem
Deep-sea hydrothermal vent fungi remain largely unexplored as crude oil degraders despite being exposed to natural seepage. Whether isolates from these extreme environments can tolerate and biotransform light and heavy crude oils was unknown.
Methods
Six fungal isolates recovered from superficial sediments of Pescadero Basin, Pescadero Transform Fault, and Alarcón Rise (Gulf of California, Mexico) hydrothermal vents were tested in tolerance and biodegradation bioassays with light crude oil (LCO) and heavy crude oil (HCO, 12° API). Species: Aspergillus terreus, A. sydowii, Aspergillus sp., Penicillium miczynskii (2 isolates), P. dodgei. Analytical methods: stable isotope analysis (δ¹³C, δ¹⁵N) and GC-FID.
Key Findings
A. terreus achieved 23% enhanced growth with LCO; C30+ hydrocarbons decreased 42% in HCO. Selectively degraded C19–C22 alkanes (38% depletion). A. sydowii showed 21% increased growth in HCO. P. miczynskii (isolate F) was the top performer overall (157% growth above controls in LCO; 53% C30+ depletion). δ¹³C enrichment (10.68–11.21‰) confirmed active carbon assimilation from crude oil.
Takeaways
Deep-sea Aspergillus terreus and A. sydowii can metabolise medium- and long-chain aliphatic hydrocarbons from crude oil, making them promising bioremediation candidates for marine oil spill contexts. Stable isotope analysis is a valuable confirmatory tool beyond gravimetric methods.
Limitations
LCO samples lacked sufficient post-incubation volume for compositional GC analysis; open system design allowed light hydrocarbon volatilisation; enzymatic mechanisms not characterised; experiments at atmospheric pressure do not reflect in-situ deep-sea conditions.

Degradation of Extra-Heavy Crude Oil by Fungi Isolated from Hydrothermal Vents in the Gulf of California Marine Aspergillus
Romero-Hernández et al. · 2025 · Mycobiology
Problem
Extra-heavy crude oil (EHCO, 7–10° API) is among the most recalcitrant petroleum fractions and no study had evaluated fungi from deep-sea hydrothermal vents specifically for EHCO bioconversion.
Methods
Eight fungal isolates from Pescadero Basin sediments (2,176–3,690 m depth) were grown on EHCO (0.5% w/v) as the sole carbon source for 15 days. Species included Aspergillus sydowii, A. terreus, Aspergillus sp., and Penicillium spp. Gravimetric analysis, GC for saturate fractions, Bradford assay for biomass, and SEM for mycelial-oil interactions.
Key Findings
Degradation table:
A. sydowii: 40.6% total EHCO / 38.3% saturate fraction degradation — best performer
P. miczynskii: 17.1% / 20.0%
A. terreus: 16.0% / 4.7%
A. sp. 4: 2.7% / −21.8% (net increase, possible biosurfactant effect)
A. sydowii "significantly outperformed" previously documented hydrocarbonoclastic fungi on EHCO. SEM revealed mycelial penetration into oil droplets, suggesting mechanical as well as enzymatic mechanisms.
Takeaways
Marine deep-sea A. sydowii is currently the best-documented fungal candidate for extra-heavy crude oil degradation, with potential relevance to bioremediation of heavy oil spills and enhanced oil recovery (EOR) strategies.
Limitations
Axenic cultures only; consortia effects unexplored. Single EHCO source, fixed concentration. Atmospheric pressure / 23°C — not deep-sea conditions. Enzymatic mechanisms not characterised. Short incubation (15 days).

Genome sequence analysis of deep sea Aspergillus sydowii BOBA1 and effect of high pressure on biodegradation of spent engine oil Marine Aspergillus
Kumar et al. · 2021 · Scientific Reports
Problem
Deep-sea fungi experience high hydrostatic pressure in situ. Whether this pressure enhances rather than inhibits hydrocarbon degradation, and which genomic elements underpin the capacity of a deep-sea Aspergillus to metabolise petroleum, were open questions.
Methods
Single isolate: A. sydowii BOBA1 from marine sediment at 3,000 m depth, Bay of Bengal, India (ITS-confirmed, 98% similarity). Hydrocarbons tested: spent engine oil (primary), plus crude oil, diesel, kerosene, petrol, n-hexadecane, toluene, xylene in growth screening. High-pressure bioreactors at 0.1 MPa and 10 MPa over 21 days. Whole genome sequencing (Illumina HiSeq 4000, 38.8 Mb, 18,932 predicted genes). FTIR, ¹H/¹³C NMR, GC-MS, and SEM.
Key Findings
Oil removal: 71.2% at 0.1 MPa vs. 82.5% at 10 MPa within 21 days — elevated pressure enhanced degradation efficiency by ~16%.
Genome analysis revealed 1,482 unique genes including cytochrome P450 monooxygenases, dioxygenases, peroxidases, glutathione S-transferases, and siderophore biosynthesis clusters, all relevant to aromatic and aliphatic hydrocarbon catabolism. Catechol, gentisate, and phthalic acid pathways confirmed. BOD reduction indicated genuine mineralisation, not just emulsification.
Takeaways
The first genomic-level characterisation of a piezotolerant deep-sea Aspergillus degrading petroleum. High pressure upregulates or preserves degradation capacity, with direct implications for in-situ bioremediation at depth. Also demonstrated growth on diesel as sole carbon source.
Limitations
Single isolate; community interactions unexplored. High-pressure limit of 10 MPa (≈1,000 m equivalent) does not represent the deepest marine environments. Long-term stability of immobilised fungal preparations not assessed.

Transcriptome Profiling Reveals Differential Gene Expression of Laccase Genes in Aspergillus terreus KC462061 during Biodegradation of Crude Oil Aspergillus
Alharbi et al. · 2022 · Biology (Basel)
Problem
Aspergillus terreus produces multiple laccase isoforms, but which specific genes drive crude oil oxidation and how their expression changes during biodegradation had not been resolved at the transcriptomic level.
Methods
Isolate KC462061 from hydrocarbon-contaminated mangrove sediment (Red Sea coast, Saudi Arabia). Substrate: 1% crude oil (aliphatic + aromatic fractions). qPCR profiling of eight laccase genes (lcc1–lcc12); copper/ABTS (Cu-ABTS) induction experiment. GC-MS for quantification of hydrocarbon breakdown. SDS-PAGE for secretome profiling. Duration: 15 days.
Key Findings
Aliphatic degradation: 82.1 ± 0.2% | Aromatic degradation: 77.4 ± 0.6%
Cu-ABTS addition increased laccase activity 22-fold. Lcc11 and Lcc12 were the predominantly upregulated genes.
Maximum laccase activity was achieved at day 7–9; aromatic ring opening confirmed by GC-MS metabolite analysis. A. terreus KC462061 was isolated from a coastal mangrove (brackish/marine-influenced) environment.
Takeaways
Laccase genes lcc11 and lcc12 are key molecular targets for engineering enhanced crude oil degradation. Copper supplementation is a practical strategy to boost Aspergillus laccase output in bioremediation applications.
Limitations
Single isolate; laboratory-controlled conditions may not reflect in-situ complexity. Coastal mangrove origin — not open-ocean marine. No field or mesocosm validation.

Fungal Extracellular Enzymes from Aspergillus spp. as Promising Candidates for Extra-Heavy Oil Degradation and Enhanced Oil Recovery Aspergillus
Zhang et al. · 2024 · Microorganisms
Problem
Extra-heavy crude oil is highly viscous and resistant to bacterial bioconversion. Whether cell-free extracellular enzyme extracts from Aspergillus could reduce EHCO viscosity and break down resin and asphaltene fractions for enhanced oil recovery was untested.
Methods
Two isolates from bitumen enrichment cultures: A. terreus HJ2 and A. nidulans HJ4. EHCO from Tahe Oilfield, Xinjiang, China (200,457 mPa·s at 50°C). Solid-state fermentation; enzyme complex E242. SARA fraction analysis, GC-MS for n-alkane redistribution, ICP-OES for heavy metal removal, biogas quantification.
Key Findings
Resin reduction: 61.8% | Asphaltene reduction: 48.0% | Viscosity decrease: 12.7%
Ni removal: 44.1% | Fe removal: 54.0% | V removal: 31.6%
Biogas (mainly CO₂ + H₂): 335.3 mL from 2 g EHCO over 7 days.
Key enzymes: dehydrogenase and catechol 2,3-dioxygenase. Cell-free approach avoids inoculation hurdles in closed reservoirs. Terrestrial isolates (non-marine) but results highly relevant to offshore EOR scenarios.
Takeaways
Extracellular enzyme preparations from A. terreus and A. nidulans can substantially degrade the heaviest petroleum fractions; the cell-free approach offers operational advantages over whole-cell bioaugmentation for in-reservoir applications.
Limitations
Non-marine isolate origin. Enzyme stability under variable reservoir conditions (temperature, salinity, pH) not characterised. No core-flooding validation. Short incubation (7 days).

Exploring Novel Fungal–Bacterial Consortia for Enhanced Petroleum Hydrocarbon Degradation Consortia context
Monteiro et al. · 2024 · Toxics
Problem
Degradation of diesel oil (B20 blend) and associated PAHs by single-strain cultures is limited. Whether consortia of novel fungi (no prior bioremediation reports) with bacteria could outperform established isolates was tested in a controlled soil microcosm design.
Methods
Isolates from diesel/biodiesel-contaminated soils (southern Brazil): 7 fungal (Penicillium, Trichoderma), 12 bacterial (Burkholderia, Bacillus, Serratia, Stenotrophomonas) species. No Aspergillus or Geotrichum isolated. Substrate: commercial diesel B20 (20% biodiesel / 80% diesel). 11 PAHs + TPH (C5–C40). 15-day incubation, GC-MS quantification.
Key Findings
Best mixed consortium (FBB1: T. koningiopsis + S. marcescens + B. cepacia): ≥91% degradation for all 11 PAHs; 93.52% TPH removal. First report of Trichoderma koningiopsis as a petroleum hydrocarbon degrader.
Fungal-only (FF1) and bacterial-only (BB1) consortia achieved ~88–94% PAH removal, comparable to the mixed consortium. Consortia consistently outperformed individual isolates.
Relevance to Aspergillus / Geotrichum
Neither genus was present in isolates from this contaminated site, illustrating that Aspergillus is not always a dominant hydrocarbon degrader in every environmental context. Provides a high-efficiency benchmark (≥91% PAH removal) for consortium performance comparison.
Limitations
Laboratory-scale only; no field or mesocosm trial. Short incubation (15 days). Cultivation parameter optimisation not explored.

Geotrichum — Literature Gap Analysis

Why Recent Literature is Absent

A systematic PubMed and open-web search covering 2021–2026 returned zero peer-reviewed original research papers specifically characterising a marine Geotrichum species as a diesel or hydrocarbon degrader. The key historical evidence consists of:

  • Geotrichum marinum Thirumalachar sp. nov. (ATCC 20614) — isolated from marine soil and patented in the 1980s (US Patent 4,415,661) for degradation of crude petroleum via emulsification, solubilisation, and consumption. This remains the most specific documented case for a marine Geotrichum.
  • Geotrichum candidum — isolated near an oil well in Hodonin, Czech Republic, and included in a patented microbial consortium (with Pseudomonas putida) for mineral oil degradation in the 1990s. No marine origin; no recent revival in the literature.
  • The 2022 review (Rahmati et al., Microorganisms, DOI: 10.3390/microorganisms10071289) on marine hydrocarbon spill bioremediation covers over 200 references and does not mention Geotrichum at all.

Interpretation: Geotrichum spp. appear in early hydrocarbon bioremediation work (pre-2000) but have been largely displaced in the literature by Aspergillus, Penicillium, Trichoderma, and Candida as model fungi. The genus is not known to be a dominant marine hydrocarbon coloniser. If research coverage of Geotrichum is needed, the most productive avenue would be to re-isolate from marine-influenced petroleum-contaminated sites and characterise using modern genomic approaches — the existing ATCC 20614 strain could itself be a starting point for a revival study.

Cross-Paper Observations

1. Methodological Patterns

  • GC/GC-MS (aliphatic fraction) and gravimetric analysis are universal quantification methods; isotope analysis (δ¹³C) is an emerging confirmatory tool (Salcedo et al. 2025).
  • Laboratory incubation periods cluster at 7–21 days, which may under-represent the degradation potential over ecologically relevant timescales.
  • Only one study (Kumar et al. 2021) combined whole-genome sequencing with degradation experiments — the dominant approach remains phenotypic/biochemical. Transcriptomics was used in Alharbi et al. 2022 but without accompanying genomics.
  • High-pressure bioreactors were used only in Kumar et al. 2021, leaving a major gap given that most marine Aspergillus isolates come from deep-sea environments (2,000–3,700 m).

2. Emerging Consensus

  • Aspergillus sydowii is the leading marine Aspergillus species for hydrocarbon degradation, outperforming other isolates on extra-heavy crude oil (Romero-Hernández et al. 2025) and achieving the highest removal rates under elevated pressure (Kumar et al. 2021).
  • Aspergillus terreus is consistently the second-ranked species, effective against medium-to-long-chain aliphatics and aromatics, with a well-characterised laccase system (Alharbi et al. 2022).
  • Hydrothermal vent isolates (Gulf of California) are emerging as a fertile source of hydrocarbonoclastic Aspergilli, likely selected by natural hydrocarbon seepage.
  • Laccase, CYP450 monooxygenases, dioxygenases, and catechol pathway enzymes are the key enzymatic machinery across all studied Aspergillus strains.

3. Open Debates

  • Volatilisation vs. true degradation: Open-system designs cannot rule out hydrocarbon evaporation contributing to measured mass loss; closed-system GC or isotope approaches are needed.
  • Pressure effects: Kumar et al. 2021 found enhanced degradation at 10 MPa; it is unclear whether this is a metabolic upregulation or a physical solubilisation effect increasing bioavailability.
  • Consortia vs. axenic: Monteiro et al. 2024 showed consortia outperform individual strains (≥91% PAH removal), yet all marine Aspergillus studies used single-strain cultures — a knowledge gap given that in situ communities include both bacteria and fungi.

4. Open Questions for Future Research

  • Can A. sydowii marine isolates be formulated into effective biosurfactant-producing consortia for open-sea oil spill response?
  • What is the in-situ degradation capacity of these fungi at actual deep-sea pressures and temperatures (2°C, >20 MPa)?
  • Are Geotrichum marinum (ATCC 20614) or newly isolated coastal Geotrichum spp. capable of degrading diesel-range hydrocarbons competitively with modern Aspergillus isolates?
  • Which specific laccase and CYP450 genes are shared across marine Aspergillus strains from geographically distinct hydrothermal vents — are there conserved "hydrocarbon degradation cassettes"?
