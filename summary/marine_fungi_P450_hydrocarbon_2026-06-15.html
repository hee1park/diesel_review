Literature Gap: Direct qPCR or enzyme assay studies of CYP P450 genes specifically in marine fungi during diesel or crude oil exposure are largely absent from 2021–2025 peer-reviewed literature. Evidence comes from three indirect routes: (1) genome/in-silico annotation of CYP gene repertoires in marine isolates, (2) metabolite profiling of PAH biotransformation products consistent with P450-mediated epoxidation, and (3) transcriptomic / gene-deletion studies in terrestrial Aspergillus whose closest relatives are marine strains. This gap is discussed in the observations section.

Paper Summaries

Genome sequence analysis of deep-sea Aspergillus sydowii BOBA1 and effect of high pressure on biodegradation of spent engine oil Marine Genomics GC-MS
Kumar et al. · 2021 · Scientific Reports · DOI: 10.1038/s41598-021-88525-9
Problem
A deep-sea Aspergillus isolated at 3,000 m in the Bay of Bengal showed remarkable crude oil degradation. Whether elevated hydrostatic pressure enhances rather than inhibits its capacity, and which genes in its genome underpin the ability, was unknown.
Methods — P450 Investigation
Whole-genome sequencing (Illumina HiSeq 4000; 38.8 Mb, 18,932 predicted genes; 1,482 genes unique to BOBA1). Genome annotated against CAZy, KEGG, and GO databases. P450-encoding and other oxidoreductase genes identified by functional annotation. Degradation confirmed by GC-MS, FTIR, and ¹H/¹³C NMR on spent engine oil, diesel, crude oil, and n-hexadecane. Pressure experiments: 0.1 MPa vs 10 MPa bioreactors.
Key Findings — P450 Related
Genome encodes: 4 cytochrome P450 monooxygenases, 6 dioxygenases, peroxidases, glutathione S-transferases, and siderophore biosynthesis clusters for aromatic hydrocarbon catabolism. Catechol, gentisate, and phthalic acid pathways confirmed — all downstream of P450-initiated ring oxidation.
Oil removal: 71.2% (0.1 MPa) → 82.5% (10 MPa) in 21 days. High pressure increased rather than suppressed activity; interpreted as enhanced solubilisation of substrates plus possible upregulation of oxidative metabolism under pressure. BOD decrease confirmed genuine mineralisation.
Takeaways
First genomic-level P450 characterisation of a piezotolerant marine Aspergillus. CYP genes are present and degradation pathways are complete, but individual CYP isoforms were not individually characterised or differentially expressed — this is the key follow-up gap.
Limitations
No qPCR, transcriptomics, or individual enzyme assays for specific CYP genes. Pressure limited to 10 MPa (≈1,000 m equivalent). Single isolate.
Robust Profiling of Cytochrome P450s (P450ome) in Notable Aspergillus spp. Genomics Phylogenomics
Dauda et al. · 2022 · Life (Basel) · DOI: 10.3390/life12030451
Problem
The CYP complement (P450ome) of ecologically diverse Aspergillus species has not been catalogued systematically, making it difficult to predict which strains harbour CYP gene families relevant to xenobiotic and hydrocarbon degradation.
Methods — P450 Investigation
Eight species profiled: A. carbonarius, A. clavatus, A. flavus, A. fumigatus, A. nidulans, A. niger, A. oryzae, A. terreus. P450 sequences retrieved from Joint Genome Institute (JGI); validated against Fungal Cytochrome P450 Database (FCPD) with conserved domain screening (e-value ≥ 7). Phylogenetic analysis via MEGA X (MUSCLE alignment). Subcellular localisation predicted by iLoc-Animal and BUSCA servers. Secondary metabolite gene cluster linkage via JGI SM database.
Key Findings — P450 Related
306 P450 enzymes total across eight species; classified into 120 families and 64 clans, spanning 15 phylogenetic clades. Multiple CYP families associated with hydrophobic xenobiotic degradation pathways. Endoplasmic reticulum is the dominant subcellular location — consistent with ω-oxidation of aliphatic hydrocarbons at the ER membrane. Secondary metabolite biosynthetic clusters linked to seven gene cluster types (PKS, NRPS, DMATS, terpene cyclases, etc.).
A. terreus and A. niger showed the highest CYP diversity; both are well-documented hydrocarbon degraders. The analysis identifies candidate CYP families for heterologous expression studies aimed at hydrocarbon biotransformation.
Takeaways
The P450ome of Aspergillus is far larger than generally appreciated; the ER-resident CYP repertoire makes these fungi intrinsically equipped for hydrocarbon oxidation. This catalogue provides a roadmap for selecting gene targets for qPCR or heterologous expression in marine hydrocarbon degradation studies.
Limitations
Entirely in silico; no marine species included; no experimental validation of CYP function. Predictions require wet-lab confirmation.
Identification of long-chain alkane-degrading (LadA) monooxygenases in Aspergillus flavus via in silico analysis Genomics In silico
Perera et al. · 2022 · Frontiers in Microbiology · DOI: 10.3389/fmicb.2022.898456
Problem
Long-chain alkane (C16–C30) degradation was considered exclusive to bacterial enzymes (bacterial LadA). Whether Aspergillus encodes eukaryotic homologs of this flavin-dependent monooxygenase family, which would explain efficient crude oil degradation without CYP52-type P450s for this substrate range, was untested.
Methods — Monooxygenase Investigation
Species: A. flavus NRRL 3357. HMMER and PSI-BLAST for homology searches against bacterial LadA sequences. MEGA X phylogenetic tree construction. SWISS-MODEL for 3D structural modelling; AutoDock Vina molecular docking with C16, C20, C24, C30 n-alkane ligands. Model validation: PROCHECK, VERIFY3D, PROSA, ERRAT. Note: This study covers LadA-type flavin monooxygenases, not CYP P450s, but both are used for initial alkane activation and are complementary systems.
Key Findings — Monooxygenase Related
First identification of eukaryotic LadA homologs. Six uncharacterised proteins detected: five classified as LadAα-type (Af1–Af5) and one LadAβ. Docking showed Af1, Af3, Af4, Af5 can capture n-alkanes up to C30 in their active pockets, with binding affinities comparable to bacterial LadA. Sequence identity 45–50% with bacterial templates.
This finding suggests Aspergillus activates very long-chain alkanes via a non-CYP mechanism alongside CYP52-mediated hydroxylation, broadening the substrate range of the genus and potentially explaining degradation of heavy crude oil fractions (C25–C30+) found in diesel range organics.
Takeaways
Fungi likely possess parallel alkane monooxygenase systems (CYP52-type P450 for C8–C18; LadA-type flavomonooxygenase for C16–C30+), making them more versatile than previously recognised. These candidate genes are prime targets for heterologous expression and enzyme kinetics studies.
Limitations
Entirely computational; no expression data or biochemical characterisation. 45–50% sequence identity with bacterial templates limits model accuracy. Classified as Frontiers (deprioritised journal) but this is the only study to identify LadA in fungi.
Ability of marine-derived fungi isolated from polluted saline environments for enzymatic hydrocarbon remediation Marine Enzyme assay
Hkiri et al. · 2023 · Brazilian Journal of Microbiology · DOI: 10.1007/s42770-023-01049-4
Problem
Marine fungi from Mediterranean saline habitats (hypersaline lakes and coastal sediments) remain largely unscreened for their enzymatic capacity to degrade crude oil under realistic salinity conditions.
Methods — Enzyme Investigation
Isolation from Lake of Bizerte, Sebkha Sijoumi, Lake of Tunis (North Tunisia). Identified species: Chaetomium jodhpurense, C. maderasense, Paraconiothyrium variabile, Phoma betae. Enzymatic assays: laccase (ABTS oxidation), lignin peroxidase (veratryl alcohol oxidation), and manganese-dependent peroxidase (MnSO₄ oxidation). Fractional Factorial design (2⁷⁻⁴) for enzyme production optimisation (pH, temperature, carbon/nitrogen source). 25-day crude oil (1% v/v in 50% seawater) degradation trial.
Key Findings — Enzymatic
Paraconiothyrium variabile was the top performer:
MnP: 2,730 U/L | LiP: 410 U/L | Laccase: 168.5 U/L | Crude oil degradation: 48.3%
P450 caveat: This study did not directly measure cytochrome P450 activity or gene expression. The dominant enzymes detected are peroxidases and laccase (ligninolytic pathway). However, the hypersaline isolation environment and the observed aromatic hydrocarbon degradation are consistent with P450-mediated initial oxidation steps followed by ligninolytic amplification — a pattern commonly inferred in other marine fungal systems (see Virués-Segovia et al. 2023 below).
Takeaways
Mediterranean saline-lake fungi show enzymatic hydrocarbon degradation comparable to better-characterised marine species. The absence of CYP measurement is a methodological gap — adding CO-difference spectroscopy or CYP-specific inhibitor assays (e.g., metyrapone) to this experimental design would confirm or quantify P450 contribution.
Limitations
No P450 measurement. Single crude oil source; four-strain panel only. No metabolite identification — impossible to confirm which enzymatic route drove degradation.
Metabolic engineering of Yarrowia lipolytica for enhanced α,ω-diol production from alkanes via CRISPR-Cas9 and CYP52 (ALK1) overexpression Genomics Enzyme assay Metabolomics
Kim et al. · 2025 · Frontiers in Bioengineering and Biotechnology · DOI: 10.3389/fbioe.2025.1695661
Problem
The marine-associated yeast Yarrowia lipolytica naturally assimilates n-alkanes via CYP52 P450 hydroxylation but over-oxidises intermediates to diacids, limiting the usefulness of its P450 machinery for selective biotransformation. Whether targeted gene deletion + CYP52 overexpression can redirect flux was untested.
Methods — P450 Investigation
CRISPR-Cas9 deletion of 14 competing pathway genes (10 in fatty alcohol oxidation, 4 in fatty aldehyde oxidation). Overexpression of P450 genes: ALK1, ALK4, ALK7 (all CYP52 family, ER-bound). Substrate: 50 mM n-dodecane. pH-controlled fed-batch fermentation. Metabolite quantification: GC-MS and HPLC for 1,12-dodecanediol and diacid byproducts. Enzyme activity: cytochrome P450 assay (CO-reduced difference spectrum for CYP52 activity).
Key Findings — CYP52 P450
ALK1 overexpression in strain YALI17 (14 competing genes deleted): 1.45 mM 1,12-dodecanediol — 29-fold increase over wild-type. pH-controlled biotransformation: 3.2 mM. CYP52 (ALK1) is the rate-limiting and product-determining enzyme for n-alkane terminal hydroxylation.
Demonstrated that CYP52 P450 gene copy number and genetic background (competing pathways) are the primary determinants of alkane conversion efficiency. Pathway metabolomics showed clean accumulation of terminal diol without ring or backbone modification — confirming ω-oxidation by CYP52 as the sole initial step.

Marine relevance: Y. lipolytica is frequently isolated from marine-derived petroleum-polluted environments; its CYP52 system is therefore directly representative of marine yeast alkane oxidation capacity.
Takeaways
CYP52 P450s (specifically ALK1) are the primary alkane-activating enzyme in yeasts that inhabit hydrocarbon-rich marine environments. The overexpression approach demonstrates that the key limitation is gene copy number and flux competition, not catalytic efficiency — relevant for designing marine bioremediation strains.
Limitations
Non-marine laboratory strain (engineered). Residual diacid overflow persists (>10 mM). No field validation; pH-controlled fermentation conditions do not reflect open-sea environments.
Marine-Derived Fungi as Biocatalysts (Review — PAH biotransformation and P450 pathway evidence) Marine Review Metabolite evidence
Virués-Segovia et al. · 2023 · Frontiers in Microbiology · DOI: 10.3389/fmicb.2023.1125639
Problem
Marine-derived fungi are diverse catalysts for biotransformation of xenobiotics, but no comprehensive synthesis had linked specific enzymatic evidence (especially P450 pathway) to measured PAH metabolites across strains from different marine habitats.
Methods — Evidence Base for P450
Systematic review of marine fungal biotransformation studies through Sep 2022. Covers fungi isolated from sponges, algae, corals, and sediments in Brazilian Atlantic and Asian coastal waters. P450 evidence types synthesised: (a) metabolite hydroxylation patterns characteristic of CYP-mediated epoxidation + epoxide hydrolase; (b) transcriptomic data (mRNA-seq) for Tolypocladium sp. CBMAI 1346; (c) inhibitor studies with P450 inhibitors in some strains.
Key Findings — P450 in Marine Fungi
Tolypocladium sp. CBMAI 1346 (sponge-derived, saline conditions): Transcriptomic analysis confirmed upregulation of cytochrome P450 monooxygenase, phenol 2-monooxygenase, and epoxide hydrolase genes during pyrene degradation. Saline-optimised conditions: 95.54% pyrene removal in 7 days. Metabolites (hydroxypyrene, pyrene dihydrodiol, dihydroxypyrene) match the canonical fungal CYP → arene oxide → trans-dihydrodiol pathway.
Aspergillus sclerotiorum CBMAI 849 (marine-derived): 99.7% pyrene / 76.6% benzo[a]pyrene removal. Metabolite analysis consistent with CYP epoxidation followed by sulfate conjugation — a unique fungal route distinct from mammalian CYP pathways.

Mucor racemosus CBMAI 847: Similarly metabolized pyrene and BaP through CYP-mediated intermediates conjugated to sulfate.
Cladosporium sp. CBMAI 1237: Produced anthraquinone as a common oxidation product from anthracene, indicating monooxygenase-driven pathway.
Takeaways
Marine fungi from sponge-associated habitats predominantly use CYP-mediated pathways for PAH degradation, producing hydroxylated / dihydrodiol / sulfate-conjugated metabolites that are diagnostic of P450 activity. Transcriptomics in Tolypocladium provides the most direct molecular evidence of P450 gene upregulation in a marine context under saline conditions.
Limitations
Review only (no new data). Lacks enzyme kinetics, specific CYP isoform identification, or gene deletion evidence linking any single CYP gene to activity. Most primary studies were conducted before 2020.
Cytochrome P450 Monooxygenase-Mediated Metabolic Utilization of Benzo[a]Pyrene by Aspergillus Species Genomics / RNAseq Transcriptomics Enzyme assay Metabolomics 2019 — key reference
Ostrem Loss et al. · 2019 · mBio · DOI: 10.1128/mBio.00558-19
Note: Published in 2019, outside the primary 2021–2025 search window. Included as the most mechanistically complete study linking a specific CYP gene to BaP metabolism in Aspergillus; directly relevant to marine isolates of A. nidulans, A. flavus, A. sydowii which share the same gene family.
Problem
Despite widespread observation that Aspergillus species degrade benzo[a]pyrene (BaP), the specific cytochrome P450 gene responsible had never been identified. Whether BaP metabolism confers a nutrient-acquisition or detoxification function was unclear.
Methods — P450 Investigation (most complete in literature)
Species: A. nidulans, A. flavus, A. oryzae, A. fumigatus. Substrate: 200 μM BaP. RNA sequencing (whole-transcriptome) of BaP-exposed vs. control cultures identified upregulated CYP candidates. Gene deletion mutagenesis: ΔbapA (AN1884) abolishes BaP degradation completely. P450 enzyme assay: Microsomal fraction + NADPH cofactor; HPLC-fluorescence detection of metabolites. HPLC metabolite profiling: Identified unique BaP metabolite distinct from mammalian CYP1A1 products. Transcription factor analysis: VeA and VelB regulate bapA under nutrient limitation.
Key Findings — P450 Gene Identified
bapA (gene AN1884, encodes CYP617D1) is the single essential P450 gene for BaP catabolism in Aspergillus. Its deletion abolishes degradation entirely. BaP degradation: 92–95% removal in 7 days. Metabolite produced differs from mammalian BaP metabolites — suggesting a novel, possibly less toxic, oxidation pathway unique to fungi.
RNAseq co-upregulated genes: citA (citrate synthase), gsdA, acuE, acuD, cycA — indicating BaP carbon assimilation into central metabolism via TCA cycle. VeA/VelB transcription factors link BaP response to nutrient status — degradation as a carbon acquisition strategy, not merely detoxification.
Takeaways
bapA/CYP617D1 is the specific P450 to target for qPCR or expression studies in marine Aspergillus isolates exposed to PAH-containing crude oil. Its homologs in marine-derived A. sydowii, A. terreus, and A. sclerotiorum are the most probable candidates for P450-driven hydrocarbon catabolism in marine environments.
Limitations
Terrestrial laboratory Aspergillus strains only; no marine isolates tested. BaP is a PAH substrate — applicability to aliphatic diesel-range hydrocarbons requires separate study (a different CYP, likely CYP52-type, would be responsible).

Cross-Paper Observations

1. CYP Families Involved by Substrate Type

  • Aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs — pyrene, BaP, phenanthrene): CYP617D1 (bapA), CYP5136A3, CYP63A2, CYP53 clan. Primary mechanism: epoxidation of aromatic ring → arene oxide → trans-dihydrodiol, often with sulfate conjugation in marine strains.
  • Medium-chain n-alkanes (C8–C18, diesel range): CYP52 family (ω-hydroxylation). Best documented in Yarrowia lipolytica (ALK1/CYP52) and predicted in Aspergillus (from P450ome profiling).
  • Long-chain alkanes (C16–C30+, heavy oil): LadA-type flavin monooxygenases (newly identified in A. flavus, Perera et al. 2022) — a non-CYP system previously thought to be bacterial only.
  • Marine context: The CYP pathway in sponge-derived fungi (reviewed in Virués-Segovia 2023) and in deep-sea Aspergillus sydowii (Kumar 2021) appears genomically complete but lacks isoform-level qPCR confirmation.

2. Methodological Patterns and Gaps

  • Genomics is established but enzyme-specific: Genome annotations identify CYP gene counts and families but do not reveal which genes are induced by specific hydrocarbons. Differential expression data (qPCR or RNAseq during oil exposure) is almost entirely absent for marine isolates.
  • Metabolite identity is indirect evidence: Trans-dihydrodiol and sulfate conjugate detection (Virués-Segovia 2023) confirms P450 pathway participation but does not identify the specific CYP gene.
  • Gene deletion is the gold standard: Ostrem Loss et al. 2019 demonstrated this definitively for BaP/CYP617D1 in terrestrial Aspergillus. No equivalent study exists for marine Aspergillus with diesel or crude oil substrates.
  • Enzyme assay (CO-difference spectrum, microsomal P450 activity): Applied in Kim et al. 2025 (Yarrowia CYP52), not yet applied to marine filamentous fungi.
  • High-pressure + P450: Kumar et al. 2021 showed oil degradation increases at 10 MPa but did not measure CYP expression — an unexplored interaction between pressure physiology and P450 regulation.

3. Emerging Consensus

  • Marine Aspergillus species (especially A. sydowii, A. terreus, A. sclerotiorum) possess complete genomic repertoires for CYP-based aromatic and aliphatic hydrocarbon catabolism, with downstream TCA-cycle assimilation pathways.
  • Fungi use a distinct P450 route from mammals: sulfate-conjugated PAH metabolites rather than reactive diol-epoxides, suggesting marine fungal P450s may produce less toxic PAH breakdown products — an important bioremediation advantage.
  • CYP52 (ω-oxidation of alkanes) and CYP617D1 (PAH epoxidation) are the two main CYP superfamilies to target in marine hydrocarbon degradation studies, but their marine-specific regulation remains uncharacterised.

4. Priority Open Questions

  • Are bapA/CYP617D1 homologs in marine A. sydowii (BOBA1) or A. sclerotiorum (CBMAI 849) transcriptionally upregulated during crude oil or diesel exposure? → qPCR study needed.
  • Do CYP52 genes in deep-sea Aspergillus show higher basal expression or induction at elevated hydrostatic pressure? → high-pressure transcriptomics needed.
  • Do the five eukaryotic LadA homologs identified in A. flavus (Perera et al. 2022) function as active alkane monooxygenases in vitro? → heterologous expression + enzyme kinetics needed.
  • Can metabolomics (LC-MS/MS) of marine fungal culture supernatants during diesel oil incubation comprehensively map the CYP vs. laccase vs. peroxidase contributions to degradation? → metabolomics study on marine isolates needed.
