## 2. Objective

Petroleum asphaltenes — the heaviest, most recalcitrant fraction of crude oil — persist in contaminated soils and resist conventional remediation. This study aimed to (1) develop a novel fungal consortium with extreme asphaltene tolerance, (2) quantify its degradation efficiency under different soil conditions, and (3) characterize the transcriptional dynamics of key hydrocarbon-degrading genes during bioremediation.

---

## 3. Methodology

**Study design:** Microcosm experiment (100 mL glass reactors, 30-day incubation at 25 °C) with three treatments:
- **T1** — Fungal consortium alone (bioaugmentation) in sterilized soil spiked with 5,000 mg asphaltenes kg⁻¹
- **T2** — Fungal consortium + native soil microbiota (bioaugmentation + biostimulation) in non-sterilized soil
- **T3** — Native soil microbiota only (biostimulation control) in non-sterilized soil

Sugarcane bagasse was added as a soil texturizer/biostimulant across all treatments.

**Consortium development:**
- 11 native filamentous fungi isolated from aged heavy crude oil–contaminated soil (Lisama oil field, Santander, Colombia) using Czapek medium with heavy crude oil as the sole carbon source
- Identified via ITS1/5.8S/ITS2 ribosomal sequencing + BLAST + neighbor-joining phylogenetic analysis (MEGA 11)
- Selected for consortium based on: tolerance to asphaltene concentrations up to 100,000 mg L⁻¹ (surface plate assays), radial growth rate, and absence of antagonism (dual culture assays)
- Final consortium: *Aspergillus oryzae* (H3), *Aspergillus sydowii* (H1), *Penicillium citrinum* (H10)

**Degradation quantification:**
- Asphaltene extraction by shaking-centrifugation (methanol:toluene 2:1) and gravimetric determination (ASTM D6560)
- CO₂ evolution measured every 48 h as a proxy for mineralization
- Consortium survival confirmed by LSSP-PCR and PDA isolation at day 30

**Gene expression analysis:**
- RT-qPCR on RNA extracted from T1 and uncontaminated control soils at days 1, 15, and 30
- Target genes: cytochrome P450 monooxygenase (*cyp*), extradiol dioxygenase (*ligB*), laccase (*tilA*), chloroperoxidase (*cpo*) in *A. oryzae*; *cyp*, *ligB*, DyP-type peroxidase (*dyp*) in *P. citrinum*
- Normalization: β-tubulin (*tub*) for *A. oryzae*; H3 histone (*H3*) for *P. citrinum*
- Relative expression calculated using the 2⁻ᐩᐩCt method
- Statistical analysis: one-way ANOVA + Tukey's test (GraphPad Prism v9.4.1, *p* < 0.05)

---

## 4. Key Results

**Asphaltene tolerance:**
- All 11 isolates tolerated up to 30,000 mg asphaltenes L⁻¹; 9 of 11 tolerated 100,000 mg L⁻¹ — exceeding previously reported fungal tolerance thresholds (30,000 mg L⁻¹)
- *Aspergillus oryzae* (H3) showed the highest radial growth rate at 100,000 mg L⁻¹

**Biodegradation efficiency:**
| Treatment | Asphaltene Degradation (wt%) |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| T1 (consortium only) | **55.62%** |
| T2 (consortium + native microbiota) | 43.70% |
| T3 (native microbiota only) | 14.89% |

- T1 significantly outperformed T2 (*p* = 0.0091), suggesting competitive interference from native microbiota
- CO₂ production peaked during the first week then declined, confirming active mineralization (not just adsorption)
- LSSP-PCR confirmed survival of all three consortium members through day 30

**Gene expression dynamics (RT-qPCR):**
- **Early phase (Day 1):** *A. oryzae cyp* upregulated (fold change = 7.5) — highest observed across all genes and timepoints
- **Mid phase (Day 15):** Peak of overall gene activity; *cyp* and *ligB* overexpressed in both *A. oryzae* and *P. citrinum*; *tilA* (laccase) and *dyp* (DyP-peroxidase) also overexpressed
- **Late phase (Day 30):** *P. citrinum cyp* and *ligB* continued overexpression; *A. oryzae* genes declined
- **Chloroperoxidase (*cpo*):** Downregulated in the presence of asphaltenes, suggesting minimal role in this system
- Pattern consistent with a **two-phase degradation model**: intracellular monooxygenation (P450s) → extracellular ligninolytic depolymerization (laccase/DyP)

## 5. Limitations & Impact

**Academic/Practical Significance:**
- First report of *A. sydowii*, *A. oryzae*, and *P. citrinum* tolerating and mineralizing high asphaltene concentrations in soil
- Provides transcriptomic evidence for the sequential, coordinated enzymatic cascade underlying fungal asphaltene degradation — a mechanistic advance over prior studies that measured degradation without gene-level resolution
- Demonstrates that high microbial tolerance is a prerequisite for effective in-soil bioremediation, and that a well-screened, non-antagonistic consortium can outperform native microbiota even at challenging contamination levels (5,000 mg kg⁻¹)
- Identifies day 15 as a critical inflection point for metabolic adaptation and peak catabolic gene expression — potentially useful for optimizing bioremediation scheduling

**Limitations:**
- *A. sydowii* gene expression was not evaluated due to absence of *cyp*, *ligB*, *dyp*, *tilA*, and *cpo* sequences in NCBI databases at the time of the study — its metabolic contribution remains uncharacterized
- Experiments were conducted in laboratory microcosms (100 mL reactors); field-scale performance under variable temperature, moisture, and soil matrix conditions is untested
- Only 7 target genes were profiled; other potentially relevant enzymes (lipases, other peroxidases, cellulases) were not measured
- The lower efficiency of T2 vs. T1 suggests negative interaction with native microbiota, but the mechanism (competition, predation, allelopathy) was not investigated
- No intermediate metabolite characterization — the degradation pathway remains inferred rather than biochemically confirmed
