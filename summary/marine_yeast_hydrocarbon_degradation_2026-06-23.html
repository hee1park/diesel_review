Padilla-Garfias et al. (2026) — Mechanisms of D. hansenii against ROS during benzo(a)pyrene biotransformation Applied & Environmental Microbiology
Authors: Francisco Padilla-Garfias, Minerva G. Araiza-Villanueva, Martha Calahorra, Norma S. Sánchez, Antonio Peña
Journal: Applied and Environmental Microbiology  |  Year: 2026
DOI: 10.1128/aem.02314-25
Problem

Benzo[a]pyrene (BaP), a carcinogenic PAH, accumulates in marine environments from petroleum spills and combustion. While Debaryomyces hansenii (a halotolerant marine yeast) is known to biotransform BaP, the underlying antioxidant mechanisms protecting the cell remain poorly understood.

Methods

Growth and viability assays at BaP concentrations up to 100 ppm; spectrofluorometric BaP quantification; RT-qPCR of 24 upregulated ORFs; enzymatic activity assays for cytochrome P450 (CYP), epoxide hydrolase (EH), and glutathione S-transferase (GST); ROS, lipid peroxidation, and protein carbonylation markers; glutathione redox status (GSH/GSSG).

Key Findings

D. hansenii degraded ~70% of BaP within 6 days at 100 ppm without loss of viability. Xenobiotic-metabolizing enzymes (CYP, EH, GST) peaked on day 3, coinciding with maximum transformation rate. Cytoplasmic and mitochondrial ROS rose sharply on day 1, triggering superoxide dismutase, catalase, and glutathione peroxidase. GSH declined and GSSG accumulated, indicating sustained oxidative pressure during biotransformation.

Takeaways

The yeast coordinates a two-pronged enzymatic network — xenobiotic metabolism plus antioxidant defense — timed to neutralize ROS generated during BaP oxidation. Its halotolerance makes it a strong candidate for marine PAH remediation.

Limitations

Laboratory-controlled conditions; single-strain study without community dynamics; metabolic endpoints beyond initial biotransformation steps are not characterized.

Vaksmaa et al. (2023) — Polyethylene degradation and assimilation by the marine yeast Rhodotorula mucilaginosa ISME Communications
Authors: Vaksmaa et al. (8 authors; Dutch & international institutions)
Journal: ISME Communications  |  Year: 2023
DOI: 10.1038/s43705-023-00267-z
Problem

Microplastic polyethylene (PE) accumulates on ocean surfaces, yet the biotic degradation contribution of marine fungi remains largely unquantified. This study tests whether R. mucilaginosa, a widespread marine yeast, can mineralize and assimilate PE-derived carbon.

Methods

Isotopic tracing with 13C-labeled polyethylene as the sole carbon source; CO2 mineralization quantified by GC/isotope ratio mass spectrometry; nanoSIMS imaging to visualize cellular incorporation of plastic-derived carbon at nanometer resolution; comparison of UV-treated versus untreated PE.

Key Findings

Substantial 13C accumulation in headspace CO2 occurred only with UV-irradiated PE (~3.8% annual degradation rate). The yeast assimilated plastic-derived carbon into biomass, confirmed by nanoSIMS; larger cells showed greater isotope enrichment. Carbon utilization proceeded even with alternative substrates present.

Takeaways

R. mucilaginosa constitutes a previously underestimated biological sink for buoyant marine PE, but photo-oxidation is a prerequisite for significant degradation. This links sunlight exposure at the ocean surface to fungal carbon cycling.

Limitations

Short incubations (<1 week); UV pre-treatment does not fully replicate natural photooxidation; single species tested; detection limit excludes degradation rates below 0.002% of added plastic.

Hegazy et al. (2024) — Isolation of Candida tropicalis B: a promising yeast for petroleum biodegradation in marine environments Microbial Cell Factories
Authors: Ghada E. Hegazy, Nadia A. Soliman, Soha Farag, Ehab R. El-Helow, Hoda Y. Yusef, Yasser R. Abdel-Fattah
Journal: Microbial Cell Factories  |  Year: 2024
DOI: 10.1186/s12934-023-02292-y
Problem

Mediterranean coastal zones are repeatedly exposed to petroleum spills, yet culturable hydrocarbon-degrading yeasts from these specific environments are undercharacterized. Autochthonous strains adapted to local salinity and temperature may outperform laboratory strains in field bioremediation.

Methods

Environmental sampling from Abu-Qir Gulf (Egypt); 18S rRNA gene sequencing for identification; HPLC for hydrocarbon quantification; Plackett-Burman and Box-Behnken statistical designs for parameter optimization; cell immobilization on wood chip carriers; scanning electron microscopy for morphology.

Key Findings

The isolate was 100% identical to Candida tropicalis. It grew across pH 4–11 (optimum pH 4) and tolerated 1–12% NaCl, reflecting broad halotolerance. Naphthalene removal reached 97.4–98.6%; phenol removal reached 52.8–79.5%. Immobilization on thin wood chips enhanced performance. Oil concentration was the dominant variable influencing degradation efficiency.

Takeaways

Extreme pH and salt tolerance combined with near-complete naphthalene removal positions this Mediterranean isolate as a practical bioaugmentation candidate for coastal oil spill response.

Limitations

Single strain from one geographic location; results may not generalize across diverse marine ecosystems or multi-component crude oil mixtures.

Padilla-Garfias et al. (2024) — Advances in PAH degradation by yeasts: a review Microorganisms (MDPI)
Authors: Francisco Padilla-Garfias, Minerva Araiza-Villanueva, Martha Calahorra, Norma S. Sánchez, Antonio Peña
Journal: Microorganisms  |  Year: 2024
DOI: 10.3390/microorganisms12122484
Problem

Six decades of research on yeast-mediated PAH degradation remain fragmented across disciplines. There is no unified synthesis of enzymatic pathways, genetic engineering potential, and omics-based discovery for yeast bioremediation.

Methods

Comprehensive narrative review spanning isolation techniques, enzymatic characterization, genetic modification approaches, and omics analyses (genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, metabolomics) across six decades of literature.

Key Findings

Marine yeasts stand out: D. hansenii degraded >80% of BaP without cell death; Rhodotorula mucilaginosa achieved 80% PAH removal under hypersaline conditions. Cytochrome P450s initiate PAH epoxidation (Phase I); epoxide hydrolases and glutathione S-transferases complete detoxification (Phases II–III). Introducing D. hansenii's DhDIT2 gene into S. cerevisiae enhanced degradation at lower temperatures.

Takeaways

Marine halotolerant yeasts are better adapted to field conditions than terrestrial strains, and genetic engineering via gene transfer (e.g., DhDIT2) enables functional optimization of chassis organisms.

Limitations

Most studies use laboratory conditions; proteomic work focuses on secretome; transcriptomic data for fungi are scarce; regulatory frameworks for GMO bioremediation remain underdeveloped.

Edgcomb et al. (2022) — Fungal hydrocarbon degradation & bacteria interactions in Guaymas Basin deep-sea sediments Frontiers in Microbiology
Authors: Virginia P. Edgcomb, Andreas P. Teske, Paraskevi Mara
Journal: Frontiers in Microbiology  |  Year: 2022
DOI: 10.3389/fmicb.2022.831828
Problem

Hydrothermal vent sediments in Guaymas Basin are rich in aliphatic and aromatic hydrocarbons, yet the contribution of fungi — particularly through interactions with anaerobic sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) — to in situ hydrocarbon cycling is unknown.

Methods

Deep-sea sampling by submersible Alvin and piston coring across temperature gradients (3–70°C); fungal ITS and 18S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing; culture-based isolation (~200 isolates); co-culture experiments pairing fungal isolates with SRB enrichments under anaerobic/microaerophilic conditions.

Key Findings

Twelve distinct Ascomycota and Basidiomycota isolates were recovered, including Cladosporium, Penicillium, Rhodotorula, and Aureobasidium spp. One Rhodotorula isolate sustained growth on naphthalene as sole carbon source under anoxic conditions for 3 weeks. The authors propose a compartmentalized degradation model: aerobic fungi initiate PAH oxidation in surficial sediments while SRB complete mineralization in sulfidic deeper layers. Biosurfactant production by fungi was documented as a mechanism to enhance hydrocarbon bioavailability.

Takeaways

Fungal-SRB syntrophy in deep-sea hydrothermal settings may represent an underappreciated pathway for complete hydrocarbon mineralization, combining the oxidative capabilities of fungi with anaerobic bacterial terminal metabolism.

Limitations

Culture-dependent methods likely miss unculturable taxa; anaerobic fungal capabilities remain poorly characterized; long-term co-culture experiments were preliminary at submission; specific metabolic interactions between the partners are not yet resolved.

Wang et al. (2024) — Engineering Saccharomyces cerevisiae microbial consortia for petroleum hydrocarbon degradation Frontiers in Bioengineering & Biotechnology
Authors: Minzhen Wang, Mengyu Zhou, Hengchang Li, Zhibei Cao, Mingzhu Ding, Yingjin Yuan
Journal: Frontiers in Bioengineering and Biotechnology  |  Year: 2024
DOI: 10.3389/fbioe.2024.1408361
Problem

Engineered yeasts expressing alkane hydroxylases degrade petroleum hydrocarbons, but marine environments impose osmotic, thermal, and acidic stress that severely reduces degradation efficiency. Combining degradative and stress-tolerant strains in consortia may overcome these limitations.

Methods

Plasmid-based integration of alkane hydroxylase genes (alkM, rubA, rubB) into S. cerevisiae (strain SAH03); GC/MS for n-hexadecane quantification; shake flask cultivation under varied osmotic, temperature, and pH conditions; glutathione quantification; co-cultivation of SAH03 with a stress-protective glutathione-secreting strain (YGSH10).

Key Findings

SAH03 alone achieved 20.42% hexadecane degradation under optimal conditions. Under 5% NaCl osmotic stress, degradation fell to 5.04% but recovered to 17.04% in the SAH03 + YGSH10 consortium. Exogenous glutathione addition confirmed that YGSH10's protective effect is mediated via redox buffering. The consortium also improved tolerance to 40°C and pH 3.0.

Takeaways

Microbial consortia that pair a degradative yeast with a stress-protective partner can largely restore petroleum degradation efficiency under marine-like osmotic conditions, offering a modular bioengineering strategy.

Limitations

Maximum hexadecane degradation remains low (<21%); the protective mechanism of the consortium versus exogenous glutathione differs and is not fully explained; not tested in true marine field conditions.

Rahmati et al. (2022) — Biotechnological approaches for marine hydrocarbon spill remediation: a review Microorganisms (MDPI)
Authors: Farzad Rahmati, Behnam Asgari Lajayer, Najmeh Shadfar, Peter M. van Bodegom, Eric D. van Hullebusch
Journal: Microorganisms  |  Year: 2022
DOI: 10.3390/microorganisms10071289
Problem

Marine oil spills remain a persistent threat and conventional clean-up methods are insufficient. A comprehensive synthesis of biotechnological strategies — biostimulation, bioaugmentation, biosurfactants, and immobilization — is needed to guide practical intervention.

Methods

Narrative literature review synthesizing microbial strains, environmental factors, and biotechnological approaches documented for marine petroleum hydrocarbon remediation.

Key Findings

Fungi including Aspergillus niger, Penicillium spp., and Cunninghamella spp. in consortia achieved ~41.5% PAH degradation enhancement. Fusarium species were noted for metabolizing chrysene under marine conditions. Biodegradation is identified as the most sustainable strategy. The review highlights that transmembrane trafficking of PAHs and related genes under high salinity remain poorly understood.

Takeaways

Multi-species fungal consortia paired with biostimulation represent a more effective marine remediation strategy than single-strain inoculation; however, unpredictable field environmental parameters remain the primary obstacle to scaling.

Limitations

Broad scope reduces mechanistic depth; field applicability of inoculated strains in open ocean systems is limited by dispersal and competition; anaerobic/high-salinity mechanisms are under-characterized.

Cross-Paper Observations

  • Cytochrome P450 as the universal initiator: All mechanistic studies (Papers 1, 2, 4) converge on CYP monooxygenases as the rate-limiting, first-attack enzyme for PAH and alkane oxidation in yeasts. CYP activity is therefore the primary engineering and screening target.
  • Halotolerance is the key selection criterion: D. hansenii and C. tropicalis (Papers 2, 3, 4) tolerate 1–12% NaCl, which mirrors typical marine salinities. The engineered S. cerevisiae consortium (Paper 6) required an exogenous stress-protection partner to function at equivalent salinities, highlighting the advantage of naturally marine-adapted isolates.
  • Photo-oxidation unlocks recalcitrant substrates: Paper 2 (Vaksmaa et al.) demonstrates that UV pre-treatment of PE is a prerequisite for significant fungal degradation, implying that surface-ocean light exposure functionally couples physical and biological weathering pathways.
  • Fungal-bacterial syntrophy in deep-sea settings: Paper 5 (Edgcomb et al.) is the only study to address anaerobic/anoxic hydrocarbon degradation by yeasts, proposing a redox-zonation model where fungi handle initial aerobic activation and SRBs complete terminal oxidation — a concept not yet tested with defined co-cultures.
  • Methodological gap — field vs. lab: All papers rely on controlled laboratory experiments or literature reviews. Not one study reports in situ field-scale bioremediation data with marine yeasts, which is the dominant unresolved challenge in the literature.
  • Open question — omics integration: Reviews (Papers 4, 7) call for transcriptomic and metabolomic studies of marine yeasts under hydrocarbon exposure, but no empirical omics dataset specifically targeting marine yeast PAH metabolism was published in this period.
