Indigenous Trichoderma asperellum biodegrades diesel-contaminated soil using a simple isolation methodology
Blanc, Duarte & Fiaux  ·  2024  ·  Environmental Pollution  ·  DOI: 10.1016/j.envpol.2024.124431
#1
Problem
Diesel fuel contamination of soil is widespread but acquiring effective native fungal bioremediation agents requires costly and complex screening procedures.
Methods
Seven filamentous fungi were isolated from diesel-contaminated soil in Rio de Janeiro (Brazil) via enrichment cultivation. Two isolates — Trichoderma asperellum and Penicillium pedernalense — were identified (morphology + ITS sequencing) and evaluated for growth on diesel-containing media using microdilution testing and GC-MS analysis of total petroleum hydrocarbons (TPH) in soil microcosms.
Findings
T. asperellum degraded 41% TPH (7 days) and 54% TPH (14 days) at 10 g diesel/kg soil; at higher concentrations (1,000 g/kg) it achieved 26%, 45%, and 48% TPH degradation over 9, 16, and 30 days. P. pedernalense showed poor tolerance at high diesel concentrations.
Takeaways
Native T. asperellum is a promising, locally adaptable agent for diesel bioremediation. The simplified enrichment method makes selection accessible for resource-limited settings.
Limitations
Lab microcosm only; no field or pilot-scale validation; TPH endpoint analysis — specific compound breakdown not fully characterised.

Indigenous Iranian fungi (Alternaria tenuissima, Epicoccum nigrum) degrade >90% of PAHs and 70% of long-chain hydrocarbons from crude oil-contaminated soil
Fallahi, Sarempour & Mirzadi Gohari  ·  2023  ·  Scientific Reports  ·  DOI: 10.1038/s41598-023-49630-z
#2
Problem
Iran hosts extensive crude oil contamination (Yadavaran oil field), and indigenous fungal bioremediation agents remain underexplored despite advantages over non-native strains.
Methods
265 fungal specimens were isolated from crude oil-contaminated soil using dilution-plate technique on PDA/MEA/WA. Genera (16) and species (25) identified via morphology and ITS sequencing. DCPIP colorimetric assay for initial screening. GC-MS quantified degradation of phenanthrene, acenaphthene, pyrene, and benzo(a)pyrene over 47 days; soil bioassay (60 days) assessed degradation of long-chain hydrocarbons (C15–C34).
Findings
35% of isolates (mainly Alternaria, Epicoccum, Neocamarosporium, Cladosporium) showed promising crude oil tolerance. Top isolates — A. tenuissima (5c-12) and E. nigrum (3b-1) — degraded >90% of all four tested PAHs. In soil assays, these isolates degraded >70% of long-chain hydrocarbons.
Takeaways
Non-white-rot hyphomycetes (Alternaria, Epicoccum) can rival basidiomycetes in PAH degradation; locality of isolation may confer ecological fitness for site-specific remediation.
Limitations
Laboratory conditions only; enzymatic pathways not elucidated; limited PAH diversity tested; no toxicity endpoints.

Native white-rot fungi from Swedish forests unlock biodegradative potential in PAH-contaminated fiberbank sediments
Hacıoğlu et al.  ·  2024  ·  Bioengineered  ·  DOI: 10.1080/21655979.2024.2396642
#3
Problem
Fiberbank sediments from historical forestry operations in northern Sweden contain persistent PAH and organic pollutant contamination. Selecting locally adapted, effective white-rot fungi (WRF) species for mycoremediation is a key bottleneck.
Methods
26 native WRF species from northern Swedish forests were screened on fiberbank material (Hagem-agar disc method) over 2 months. Growth capacity and PAH removal efficiency were assessed for both low-MW (2–3 ring) and high-MW (4–6 ring) PAHs.
Findings
Top colonisers: Laetiporus sulphureus (72%), Hymenochaete tabacina (68%), Diplomitoporus crustulinus (67%). For 2–3 ring PAHs, Phellinus punctatus achieved 68% removal; for heavier 4–6 ring PAHs, D. crustulinus achieved 44% removal. No single species excelled across all pollutant classes.
Takeaways
Native WRF sourcing outperforms exotic strains for site-specific PAH contamination. Selecting species based on pollutant molecular weight improves outcomes; consortia of complementary species may be optimal.
Limitations
Controlled lab assay; specific PAH degradation rates not quantified; enzymatic mechanisms not explored; fiberbank matrix may limit generalisability to other soil types.

A novel Ciboria sp. achieves up to 99% TPH depletion in oil-refinery soil via mycoaugmentation and fungal–bacterial synergy
Becarelli et al.  ·  2021  ·  Frontiers in Microbiology  ·  DOI: 10.3389/fmicb.2021.647373
#4
Problem
Soils at abandoned petroleum refineries accumulate persistent total petroleum hydrocarbon (TPH) contamination that resists conventional remediation; a novel fungal agent and understanding of fungal–bacterial interplay could improve outcomes.
Methods
A novel Ciboria sp. (Ascomycota) was isolated from hydrocarbon-contaminated soil at an abandoned Italian oil refinery. Mesocosm experiments (90 days) with 1% and 7% fungal inoculant levels. GC-MS quantified TPH depletion. 16S rDNA and ITS metabarcoding tracked microbial community dynamics; functional metagenomics characterised degradation gene pools.
Findings
1% inoculant achieved 78% TPH depletion; 7% inoculant achieved 99% TPH depletion after 90 days. Ciboria sp. facilitated succession from generalist saprophytic bacteria (Streptomyces, Nocardoides, Pseudonocardia) toward hydrocarbon-specialist bacteria via mycelial network "highways."
Takeaways
Mycoaugmentation with a novel Ascomycete can near-completely deplete refinery TPH by reshaping indigenous bacterial communities — the fungus acts as an ecological engineer, not merely a degrader.
Limitations
Single fungal species; mesocosm scale only; inoculant production for field application not addressed; TPH endpoints don't resolve individual compound fates.
Tags
Crude Oil / TPH Ciboria sp. Fungal–Bacterial Synergy Mycoaugmentation

Scale-up of mycoremediation for aliphatic hydrocarbons: key factors, challenges, and field-deployment strategies
Antón-Herrero et al.  ·  2023  ·  Journal of Fungi  ·  DOI: 10.3390/jof9121205
#5
Problem
Despite strong lab-scale results, mycoremediation of petroleum hydrocarbons rarely translates to field-scale effectiveness; the factors governing this scale-up gap are poorly synthesised.
Methods
Comprehensive narrative review drawing from published literature, patent databases, and bibliometric analyses. Covered fungal species selection, inocula formulation, bulking agent choice, bacterial–fungal interactions, contaminant bioavailability, environmental controls, surfactant use, and mycopile engineering.
Findings
Ascomycota genera (Aspergillus, Penicillium, Fusarium) outperform white-rot basidiomycetes in field conditions due to environmental robustness. Mycelial networks act as bacterial dispersal "highways." Spent mushroom substrate (SMS) is a cost-effective inoculant. Mycoremediation can achieve removal efficiencies comparable to thermal desorption. Contaminant bioavailability (assessed via mild solvent extraction) is the key rate-limiting factor at scale.
Takeaways
Field-scale success requires bioavailability assessment, adaptive species selection, optimised bulking agents, and managed environmental parameters. Lab-scale results should never be extrapolated directly to field scale without pilot studies.
Limitations
Review only — no original experimental data; limited pilot/field-scale data exists for aliphatic hydrocarbons specifically; surfactant effects for alkanes remain largely untested.
Tags
Review Scale-Up Mycopile Aspergillus / Penicillium

Aspergillus oryzae and Mucor irregularis from Nigerian crude oil-polluted sites degrade engine oil hydrocarbons via laccase and peroxidase enzymes
Asemoloye et al.  ·  2020  ·  Microorganisms  ·  DOI: 10.3390/microorganisms8121912
#6
Problem
Nigeria's Niger Delta experiences chronic crude oil pollution; characterising enzymatic mechanisms of locally isolated ascomycetes is essential for developing effective bioremediation applications.
Methods
Fungi isolated from crude oil-contaminated Nigerian soils via serial dilution on PDA. ITS sequencing and phylogenetic analysis (MEGA 7.0). Colorimetric enzyme assays measured laccase (Lac), manganese peroxidase (MnP), and lignin peroxidase (LiP). Growth on mineral salt medium with engine oil (complex BTEX + PAH mixture) as sole carbon source. Dose inhibition response (DIR) assessed at concentrations up to 20%.
Findings
Both fungi survived 20% hydrocarbon concentrations. DIR: M. irregularis 45.85%, A. oryzae 39.40% (indicating greater tolerance in A. oryzae). A. oryzae degraded a broad hydrocarbon spectrum; M. irregularis preferentially targeted long-chain hydrocarbons and BTEX. Laccase activity exceeded MnP and LiP in both strains.
Takeaways
Laccase-dominated enzymatic profiles in non-white-rot ascomycetes can drive effective hydrocarbon degradation, broadening the candidate organism pool for mycoremediation beyond basidiomycetes.
Limitations
No quantitative GC-MS degradation data for specific compounds; soil microcosm studies absent; enzyme production under field-relevant conditions not tested.
Tags
Aspergillus Mucor Laccase / MnP Engine Oil / BTEX

Fungal delignification and organic waste amendment enhance PAH remediation in soil: a comprehensive review
Omoni et al.  ·  2024  ·  Indian Journal of Microbiology  ·  DOI: 10.1007/s12088-024-01383-7
#7
Problem
PAH-contaminated soils, especially nutrient-poor ones, respond poorly to standard bioremediation; combining white-rot fungi with organic waste amendments may overcome persistent bioavailability and nutrient barriers.
Methods
Review synthesising literature on white-rot basidiomycetes (Trametes versicolor, Phanerochaete chrysosporium, Pleurotus ostreatus, Bjerkandera adusta) combined with organic amendments (corn cobs, spent brewery grains, compost, biochar). Reviewed fungal immobilisation on lignocellulosic carriers and enzyme induction strategies (laccase, MnP, LiP).
Findings
Corn cob amendment achieved 16–44% PAH degradation; spent brewery grains achieved 48.7% phenanthrene removal; immobilised P. ostreatus achieved 61.2% degradation efficiency. Lower-MW PAHs consistently degraded more effectively than higher-MW compounds. Organic amendments enhanced enzyme induction and fungal colonisation.
Takeaways
Coupling lignocellulosic organic waste with white-rot fungi creates dual benefit: nutrient supply for fungi + co-substrate induction of ligninolytic enzymes. Immobilisation on carriers protects mycelium in hostile soil environments.
Limitations
Review only; field-scale data scarce; variability in PAH types and soil matrices complicates cross-study comparison; long-term persistence of amendments not evaluated.

Cunninghamella echinulata and Mucor circinelloides degrade 59.7% and 78.1% of crude oil via biosurfactant production
Yehia  ·  2023  ·  Brazilian Journal of Microbiology  ·  DOI: 10.1007/s42770-023-01008-z
#8
Problem
Crude oil spills in arid environments require locally adapted fungal agents; Zygomycetes are underexplored compared to basidiomycetes and ascomycetes for petroleum bioremediation.
Methods
Fungi isolated from petroleum-polluted soil in Al Jubail, Saudi Arabia. ITS and EF-1α gene sequencing for species identification. Crude oil (1% v/v) as sole carbon source in mineral salt medium. GC quantification of crude oil residuals. Biosurfactant recovery via acid precipitation, solvent extraction, and salting-out methods. Surface tension and emulsification index (E24) measured. DCPIP colorimetric screening. Laccase and peroxidase enzyme assays.
Findings
Cunninghamella echinulata (YS-6) removed 59.7%; Mucor circinelloides (YS-10) removed 78.1% of crude oil. Surface tension reduced to 41.2 and 35.9 mN/m respectively. Emulsification indices ranged 36.7–53.4%. Biosurfactant yields up to 4.6 g/L by solvent extraction. Laccase activity dominated over MnP and LiP.
Takeaways
Biosurfactant production by Zygomycetes significantly enhances crude oil emulsification and bioavailability, a mechanism often overlooked compared to enzymatic degradation pathways.
Limitations
Liquid culture only; no soil matrix experiments; specific hydrocarbon fractions degraded not resolved by GC-MS; long-term stability of biosurfactants not assessed.
Tags
Cunninghamella Mucor Crude Oil Biosurfactant

Native fungal isolates and consortia achieve up to 99.18% saturated hydrocarbon degradation in petroleum-contaminated systems
Hamad et al.  ·  2021  ·  The Scientific World Journal  ·  DOI: 10.1155/2021/6641533
#9
Problem
Individual fungal isolates face limitations in degrading the full complexity of petroleum hydrocarbons; whether fungal consortia offer synergistic benefits over single-strain inoculation is insufficiently quantified.
Methods
Six fungal species (Curvularia brachyspora, Penicillium chrysogenum, Scopulariopsis brevicaulis, Cladosporium sphaerospermum, Alternaria alternata, Stemphylium botryosum) and three consortia (FC1: P. chrysogenum+C. brachyspora; FC2: S. brevicaulis+S. botryosum; FC3: S. brevicaulis+S. botryosum+C. sphaerospermum) were evaluated for petroleum hydrocarbon degradation using GC and HPLC analysis of n-paraffins, isoparaffins, PAHs, and saturated fractions.
Findings
C. sphaerospermum: 97.19% n-paraffin and 98.88% isoparaffin degradation. A. alternata: 72.07% total PAH degradation. FC1 consortium: 86.57% saturated hydrocarbon degradation. FC2 consortium: 99.18% saturated hydrocarbon degradation — highest of all treatments. All consortia outperformed individual isolates.
Takeaways
Fungal consortia consistently outperform individual isolates; complementary substrate ranges within consortia create functional diversity that covers the full spectrum of petroleum hydrocarbon classes.
Limitations
Liquid culture experiments only; soil matrix effects not tested; mechanisms of inter-species synergy unexplored; no toxicity or ecotoxicity endpoints reported.

Fungal Consortia Penicillium / Alternaria PAH / n-Paraffins Synergy

Fusarium species from Saudi oil reservoirs degrade diesel, crude oil, and kerosene while producing non-toxic biosurfactants
Al-Otibi, Al-Zahrani & Marraiki  ·  2023  ·  Journal of Fungi  ·  DOI: 10.3390/jof9020216
#10
Problem
Fusarium species are common in petroleum-contaminated sites but their multi-substrate hydrocarbon degradation capacity and biosurfactant production relevant to bioremediation are not well characterised across diesel, kerosene, and mixed oils.
Methods
Three Fusarium spp. (F. verticillioides, F. proliferatum, F. oxysporum) isolated from Saudi oil reservoir soil. Growth on crude oil, used oil, diesel, kerosene, and mixed oil as sole carbon sources. DCPIP colorimetric assay; scanning electron microscopy for morphological assessment; drop-collapse, oil-spreading, and emulsification assays for biosurfactant activity. Four biosurfactant recovery protocols applied. Tomato seed germination toxicity assay.
Findings
F. verticillioides showed highest biodegradation against used oil (OD 0.557, day 15); F. oxysporum excelled on used oil (OD 0.436). F. proliferatum achieved highest emulsification (63%) on mixed oil. Biosurfactant yields up to 4.6 g/L (F. verticillioides) by solvent extraction. All biosurfactants were non-toxic (positive plant germination assay).
Takeaways
Fusarium-derived biosurfactants are non-toxic and environment-friendly adjuvants; their combination with in situ Fusarium degradation offers an integrated, locally-sourced bioremediation strategy for arid petroleum-contaminated soils.
Limitations
No GC-MS quantification of actual hydrocarbon degradation; OD-based metabolic activity is a proxy, not a direct measure of compound removal; no soil microcosm or field data.
Tags
Fusarium Diesel / Crude Oil / Kerosene Biosurfactant Ecotoxicity

Cross-Paper Observations

Methodological Patterns

  • DCPIP colorimetric assay is the most widely used high-throughput screening tool for initial hydrocarbon degradation potential (Papers 2, 8, 10), though it is a proxy measure and not compound-specific.
  • GC-MS / GC remains the gold standard for quantitative TPH and individual compound degradation, but is not always used — several studies (6, 10) relied solely on metabolic activity proxies, limiting interpretability of degradation claims.
  • ITS gene sequencing is universal for fungal identification; EF-1α (Paper 8) adds resolution for difficult-to-distinguish genera.
  • Studies vary widely in scale: liquid enrichment media (Papers 6, 8, 10), soil microcosms (Papers 1, 2, 4), and mesocosms (Paper 4). Field or pilot-scale data remain exceptionally rare (Paper 5 review highlights this gap).

Consensus Points

  • Laccase dominance: Across diverse fungal genera (Aspergillus, Mucor, Cunninghamella, Fusarium), laccase consistently exceeds MnP and LiP activity, suggesting it is a primary enzymatic driver of hydrocarbon oxidation even outside white-rot basidiomycetes.
  • Consortia outperform isolates: Papers 4 and 9 demonstrate that mixed fungal–bacterial or multi-species fungal communities achieve higher degradation rates than single strains, attributed to complementary substrate ranges and metabolic diversity.
  • Biosurfactant production enhances degradation: Papers 8 and 10 both highlight biosurfactant-mediated emulsification as a key mechanism for improving hydrocarbon bioavailability — often overlooked versus enzymatic pathways.
  • Native isolate advantage: Papers 1, 2, 3, and 8 all emphasise locally isolated fungi as superior candidates due to ecological pre-adaptation to site-specific contaminant profiles and environmental conditions.

Active Debates & Open Questions

  • Ascomycetes vs. Basidiomycetes at field scale: Paper 5 argues Ascomycota (Aspergillus, Penicillium, Fusarium) outperform white-rot basidiomycetes in field conditions despite the latter being more studied in labs. Direct field comparison studies are lacking.
  • High-MW PAH degradation bottleneck: Papers 3 and 7 report that 4–6 ring PAHs are consistently harder to degrade. Whether this is enzymatic capacity or bioavailability is unresolved.
  • Ecological risk of introduced fungi: Only Paper 10 tested ecotoxicity (plant germination assay). Broader ecological impacts of mycoaugmentation — including effects on native microbial communities — are systematically ignored across this literature.
  • Quantification standards: Lack of consistent endpoints (TPH vs. PAH classes vs. specific compounds) makes cross-study comparison difficult and suggests need for standardised reporting protocols.
