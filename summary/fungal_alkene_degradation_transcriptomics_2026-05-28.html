Metatranscriptomics of Fungal–Bacterial Consortia in Aircraft Kerosene Tanks Identifies Fuel-Degrading Genes
Krohn et al.  ·  Front. Microbiology 2021  ·  Metatranscriptomics Kerosene / Alkenes Multi-species
Problem
Microbial contamination of aviation fuel tanks causes corrosion and potential fuel degradation. The genetic basis for fuel metabolism — including alkene components — by resident fungal-bacterial biofilms was unknown.
Methods
Six biofilm samples from aircraft kerosene tanks were subjected to metagenomic sequencing (Illumina NextSeq 500; 33–168 M reads/sample; metaSPAdes assembly + metaBAT2 binning). One biofilm sample (triplicate) underwent metatranscriptomic analysis: ~112 M bacterial + 130 M fungal reads mapped to reference genomes after rRNA depletion.
Findings
Fungal transcriptomics (predominantly Amorphotheca spp.) revealed 15,441 RNA reads mapped to aldehyde dehydrogenases and cytochrome P450-like enzymes — the central alkene/alkane oxidation pathway. Notably, 4,322 reads mapped to carboxylesterase type II in Amorphotheca, enabling long-chain hydrocarbon ester hydrolysis. Alkene reductase gene expression was also detected, indicating in-situ reductive desaturation activity in fuel.
Takeaways
Metatranscriptomics from a real industrial environment demonstrates that fungi actively express alkene/alkane catabolism genes in situ. CYP450-aldehyde dehydrogenase cascades and carboxylesterases are the dominant active enzymatic routes for fuel alkene metabolism.
Limitations
Only one sample processed for transcriptomics due to restricted tank access; poor genome annotation for Amorphotheca constrained functional gene interpretation; no quantitative degradation measurement.
Transcriptomics Identifies 153 Degradation Enzymes in Alternaria alternata FB1 Degrading Polyethylene
Gao, Liu & Sun  ·  J. Hazardous Materials 2022  ·  RNA-seq Polyethylene Marine fungus
Problem
Polyethylene (PE) — one of the most abundant plastic pollutants — is highly recalcitrant due to its long polyene chains. The molecular enzymatic basis for fungal PE degradation remains almost entirely uncharacterized.
Methods
Isolation of Alternaria alternata FB1 from marine plastic waste. Degradation quantified by SEM, FTIR, XRD, and GC-MS over 120 days. Transcriptomic profiling performed with PE film as sole carbon source to identify differentially expressed enzymes; two candidate enzymes (laccase, peroxidase) were recombinantly expressed and functionally validated.
Findings
After 120 days, A. alternata FB1 reduced PE molecular weight by 95%; the primary degradation product was diglycolamine (93.28% by GC-MS). Transcriptomics identified 153 upregulated enzymes potentially involved in PE degradation, including laccase (59.49 kDa) and peroxidase (36.7 kDa). Both recombinant proteins demonstrated direct PE alkene bond oxidation activity in vitro.
Takeaways
Transcriptomics is a powerful prospecting tool for cataloguing the broad enzymatic repertoire fungi deploy against polyene substrates. Laccase and peroxidase acting on the C=C bonds introduced by UV pre-oxidation are the verified initial attack enzymes in Alternaria PE catabolism.
Limitations
PE requires UV pre-treatment to introduce surface C=C bonds and functional groups before fungal attack begins; no untreated PE control reported; downstream metabolic pathway from diglycolamine onward not characterized.
qRT-PCR Transcriptomics of Laccase Gene Family in Aspergillus terreus during Crude Oil Degradation
Alharbi et al.  ·  Biology (Basel) 2022  ·  qRT-PCR Crude oil / aromatics Gene family
Problem
Fungal laccases oxidize both aliphatic and aromatic hydrocarbon substrates — including alkene π-bonds — but which of the multiple laccase paralogues is transcriptionally active during crude oil biodegradation was unknown.
Methods
Cultivation of A. terreus KC462061 on crude oil with five inductors (Cu²⁺, Zn²⁺, ABTS, ferulic acid, guaiacol) in factorial design. Laccase activity assayed spectrophotometrically; hydrocarbon degradation by GC-MS; protein profiles by SDS-PAGE; qRT-PCR quantification of eight laccase paralogs: Lcc1, 3, 5, 6, 8, 10, 11, 12.
Findings
Cu–ABTS co-induction increased laccase yield 22-fold after 10 days; aliphatic hydrocarbon degradation reached 82.1% and aromatic degradation 77.4%. Among eight paralogues, Lcc11 and Lcc12 dominated the transcription profile and their expression was tightly coupled to Cu-ABTS induction — while Lcc1 and Lcc3 remained constitutively low.
Takeaways
Laccase gene subfunctionalization is real: within an eight-member family, only two paralogues drive the hydrocarbon-degradation response. Cu²⁺-ABTS induction is the dominant regulatory signal for alkene-attacking laccases in Aspergillus.
Limitations
Single isolate; real-world soil conditions not tested; full transcriptome-wide analysis not performed (only eight pre-selected laccase genes); regulatory mechanism upstream of Lcc11/12 uncharacterized.
RNA-Seq of Phlebia acerina Reveals Transferase and Binding-Protein Upregulation during BaP Degradation
Zhang et al.  ·  J. Fungi 2023  ·  RNA-seq Benzo[a]pyrene White-rot fungus
Problem
Benzo[a]pyrene (BaP) is a five-ring PAH with dense conjugated π-electron (alkene) character, classified as a Group 1 human carcinogen. The genes and enzymes deployed by white-rot fungi for BaP catabolism had not been profiled by RNA-seq.
Methods
Liquid culture assays of Phlebia acerina with BaP (50 mg/L); three nonionic surfactants (Brij 30, Triton X-100, Tween 80) tested for bioavailability enhancement; enzyme activity assays (LiP, MnP, laccase); RNA-seq transcriptomics applied to BaP-exposed cultures; qPCR validation of key differentially expressed transcripts.
Findings
P. acerina achieved 57.7% BaP degradation without surfactant; Brij 30 (1–2 g/L) boosted this to ~94%. RNA-seq revealed upregulation of transferase genes and binding proteins as the primary transcriptional response — not CYP450 (CYP450 inhibitor reduced degradation only ~6%). Peroxidases remained the main catalytic drivers; Brij 30 suppressed laccase but preserved peroxidase activity.
Takeaways
CYP450 is surprisingly dispensable for high-ring PAH degradation by Phlebia; transferases and binding proteins dominate the transcriptomic signature, suggesting that conjugation and solubilization — not initial oxidation — are the transcriptionally costly steps. Surfactant selection critically shapes both enzymatic and gene-expression outcomes.
Limitations
High BaP concentration (50 mg/L) is supra-environmental; single strain; BaP mineralization (CO₂ production) not directly measured; specific transferase gene identities not fully characterized.
Genomic and Transcriptomic Screening of Penicillium digitatum for Limonene (Cyclic Alkene) Biotransformation Genes
Zhang et al.  ·  Front. Microbiology 2020  ·  RNA-seq Limonene / terpene CYP450
Problem
Limonene — a bicyclic monoterpene containing two C=C bonds — is converted to the high-value fragrance α-terpineol by Penicillium digitatum, but the responsible enzyme(s) and transcriptional regulation were unknown.
Methods
Whole-genome sequencing (29.09 Mb, 9,086 genes); transcriptomic comparison of limonene-treated vs. control cultures at 12 h (Illumina RNA-seq); differential gene expression analysis; enzyme inhibitor studies (CYP450 inhibitor, NADPH depletion); subcellular fractionation; qPCR validation of top candidates.
Findings
4,128–4,148 differentially expressed genes identified under limonene exposure. Two CYP450 genes emerged as top candidates: PDIDSM_85260 and PDIDSM_67430 (CYP450 reductase; 132-fold upregulated). ABC transporters and terpenoid synthase genes were also upregulated; TCA cycle and sterol biosynthesis genes were downregulated, suggesting redirection of acetyl-CoA flux toward limonene catabolism. CYP450 inhibitor confirmed P450 is the rate-limiting oxidase for C=C epoxidation in limonene → α-terpineol conversion.
Takeaways
CYP450 epoxidation of the exocyclic C=C bond is the decisive catalytic step, with the reductase partner gene as the rate-limiting transcriptional target. Mass gene reprogramming (>4,000 DEGs) in response to a single alkene suggests global metabolic rerouting, not a narrow dedicated pathway.
Limitations
Functional role of top CYP450 candidate PDIDSM_85260 confirmed by inhibitor studies but not by knockout; proteomic data did not fully corroborate all predicted protein expressions, pointing to significant post-transcriptional regulation.
Multi-omics (RNAseq + Secretome) of Aspergillus parasiticus on Long-Chain Alkanes for PE Modification
Siaperas et al.  ·  Environmental Pollution 2025  ·  RNA-seq + Proteomics Long-chain alkanes / PE Multi-omics
Problem
Long-chain alkanes (C24–C36) and polyethylene share structural features but few fungi have been molecularly characterized for their degradation; the secreted enzymatic arsenal remains poorly defined at the omics level.
Methods
Isolation of A. parasiticus MM36 from Tenebrio molitor gut; growth on C16 (hexadecane) or C24–C36 long-chain alkane (lcAlk) mixtures; genome sequencing and annotation; RNAseq at multiple time points comparing C16 vs. lcAlk; secretome profiling by LC-MS/MS to identify extracellular enzymes; PE modification assays using culture supernatants.
Findings
RNAseq revealed enrichment of oxidoreductase activities and carboxylic acid metabolism on lcAlk vs. C16; transmembrane transporters dominated day-2 responses; biosynthetic processes dominated day-3. Secretome LC-MS/MS identified key enzymes: multicopper oxidases, peroxidases, unspecific peroxygenase, and FAD-dependent monooxygenases. Hydrophobins and hydrophobic surface-binding proteins facilitated substrate contact. A model proposed: extracellular oxyfunctionalization → C=C functionalized intermediates → uptake via surface proteins → membrane transporters → intracellular catabolism.
Takeaways
The combination of RNAseq and secretome proteomics provides orthogonal evidence for the alkene functionalization cascade. Unspecific peroxygenase is an underappreciated enzyme family for initial C–H/C=C oxidation in long-chain substrates; hydrophobins are not merely structural but are transcriptionally regulated components of the uptake machinery.
Limitations
MM36 is from an insect gut — ecological relevance to open-environment contaminated soils is unclear; PE modification verified but complete mineralization not demonstrated; secretome only captures extracellular proteins, missing intracellular catabolic enzymes.
Integrative Transcriptome + Metabolome of Amylostereum areolatum for Terpenoid (Multi-alkene) Biosynthesis Genes
Wang et al.  ·  J. Fungi 2025  ·  RNA-seq + Metabolomics Terpenoids / sesqui- & triterpenoids Multi-omics
Problem
Terpenoids — the most structurally diverse class of natural products, all containing polyene/alkene backbones — are produced by Amylostereum areolatum, a mutualist of the wood wasp Sirex noctilio, but the biosynthetic gene network and metabolite profile were completely unknown.
Methods
Integrated transcriptome (Illumina NovaSeq) and metabolome (LC-MS + SPME-GC-MS) analysis across three fungal developmental stages (7, 14, and 21 days); differential expression analysis; enzyme–metabolite correlation; pathway mapping to KEGG terpenoid biosynthesis routes.
Findings
103 terpenoids identified (triterpenoids and sesquiterpenoids most diverse). 26 biosynthesis-associated genes detected; 11 showed differential expression across developmental stages. Key regulators: HMGS1 and HMGR2 (MVA pathway rate-limiting steps) and AaTPS1–3 (terpene synthases catalyzing the initial alkene-forming cyclisation reactions). All three TPS genes showed temporal peak expression at 14 days.
Takeaways
The terpene synthase step — which forms the initial C=C bond in the terpenoid skeleton — is the coordinated transcriptional hub. HMGS1/HMGR2 upstream regulation of MVA flux is transcriptionally separable from the downstream TPS alkene-forming step. Multi-omics enables correlation of transcript dynamics to metabolite accumulation, confirming enzymatic bottlenecks.
Limitations
Definitive structural confirmation of terpenoid identities requires NMR; mass spectrometry–based IDs carry isomeric ambiguity. Functional validation of AaTPS1–3 by heterologous expression is pending.

Cross-Paper Observations

Methodological Patterns

  • Illumina short-read RNA-seq (NextSeq, NovaSeq) is the universal transcriptomic platform across all studies.
  • Multi-omics integration (RNAseq + proteomics/metabolomics) is the emerging gold standard: Siaperas 2025 and Wang 2025 both combine transcriptomics with a second omics layer for orthogonal validation.
  • qRT-PCR of candidate gene families (Alharbi 2022) remains practical when a targeted gene family is the focus rather than discovery-mode profiling.
  • GC-MS / LC-MS/MS provides the chemical evidence tying transcriptomic findings to actual degradation products.

Consensus Enzyme Findings

  • CYP450s are upregulated in every aerobic alkene-degradation system — they catalyze the initial epoxidation of C=C bonds.
  • Multicopper oxidases / laccases appear consistently for aromatic and polyene substrates (Gao 2022, Zhang 2023, Siaperas 2025).
  • Aldehyde dehydrogenases (terminal oxidation step) co-expressed with CYP450 in fuel degradation (Krohn 2021).
  • Terpene synthases (TPS) form the alkene backbone de novo — the reverse of degradation, but governed by the same carbon-skeleton C=C chemistry (Wang 2025).

Active Debates

  • CYP450 centrality vs. peroxidase-first models: CYP450 inhibitor reduced BaP degradation by only 6% in Phlebia acerina (Zhang 2023) — contrasting with Penicillium digitatum where CYP450 is rate-limiting for limonene epoxidation (Zhang 2020). Substrate ring number and fungal lineage may dictate which enzyme family leads.
  • Gene expression vs. protein secretion: Siaperas 2025 found mismatch between RNAseq-predicted enzymes and secretome-detected proteins, echoing the disconnect reported in Penicillium (Zhang 2020). Post-transcriptional regulation is under-studied.
  • Laccase role in alkene attack: Some studies position laccase as a direct alkene oxidase; others assign it a peripheral depolymerisation or mediator-cycling role.

Open Questions

  • Complete metabolic pathway reconstruction for terminal alkene (C=C) degradation — intermediates between initial oxidation and TCA-cycle entry remain uncharacterized in most fungi.
  • The role of unspecific peroxygenases (UPOs) in alkene epoxidation — identified by Siaperas 2025 but absent from earlier studies; likely underdetected.
  • Why do some fungi (Phlebia, Aspergillus) require thousands of gene-expression changes to degrade a single alkene substrate? Stress response co-activation vs. genuine metabolic rewiring.
  • Ecological context: are these transcriptional programs replicated in field soils, or are they laboratory artefacts of high substrate concentrations?
  • Substrate specificity determinants in TPS and CYP52/CYP450 enzymes — what governs alkene chain-length and ring-structure preference?

Alkene Substrate Coverage

  • Terminal/chain alkenes: Kerosene (Krohn 2021), polyethylene (Gao 2022, Siaperas 2025)
  • Cyclic alkene terpenes: Limonene (Zhang 2020), sesqui/triterpenoids (Wang 2025)
  • Aromatic PAH alkenes: Crude oil aromatics (Alharbi 2022), benzo[a]pyrene (Zhang 2023)
  • Notably absent: vinyl chloride, styrene, isoprene — open niches for future transcriptomic study.

Key Genes / Gene Families Identified

  • PDIDSM_67430 (CYP450 reductase, 132× up) — limonene C=C epoxidation (P. digitatum)
  • Lcc11, Lcc12 — crude oil-responsive laccase paralogues (A. terreus)
  • HMGS1, HMGR2, AaTPS1–3 — MVA + terpene synthase hub (A. areolatum)
  • Aldehyde dehydrogenases + CYP450 — fuel alkene oxidation in Amorphotheca
  • Multicopper oxidases, unspecific peroxygenase, FAD-monooxygenases — long-chain alkene functionalization (A. parasiticus)
