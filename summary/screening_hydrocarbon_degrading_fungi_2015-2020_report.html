Gargouri et al. (2015) — Isolation and characterization of hydrocarbon-degrading yeast strains from petroleum-contaminated industrial wastewater BioMed Research International
Authors: Boutheina Gargouri, Najla Mhiri, Fatma Karray, Fathi Aloui, Sami Sayadi
Journal: BioMed Research International  |  Year: 2015
DOI: 10.1155/2015/929424
Problem

Petroleum contamination in industrial wastewater from refineries requires cost-effective biological treatment. Yeasts are underexplored relative to bacteria for hydrocarbon bioremediation, and indigenous strains adapted to local conditions may offer advantages over exogenous inocula.

Methods

Selective enrichment culture in a continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR) with refinery wastewater (Sfax, Tunisia) as inoculum; mineral salt medium with crude oil as sole carbon source; 5 yeast strains isolated; final 2 selected (HC1, HC4) for detailed characterization by ITS sequencing; total petroleum hydrocarbon (TPH) removal measured by gravimetry; n-alkane profiles by GC.

Key Findings

HC1 was identified as Candida tropicalis; HC4 as Trichosporon asahii. Both achieved >95% TPH removal (C. tropicalis: 97%, T. asahii: 95%). Degradation was concentrated on n-alkanes C11–C26; aromatic hydrocarbons were not efficiently metabolized as sole carbon sources. Cell surface hydrophobicity (CSH) positively correlated with uptake efficiency.

Takeaways

CSTR-based enrichment effectively selects for high-performing indigenous yeasts, and CSH is a practical proxy for screening degradation potential. However, the alkane-only degradation profile leaves aromatic fractions of crude oil unaddressed.

Limitations

Laboratory-scale results; no field validation; aromatic hydrocarbon degradation remained incomplete; long-term performance under fluctuating environmental conditions not evaluated.

Barnes et al. (2018) — Bioremediation potential of hydrocarbon-utilizing fungi from select marine niches of India 3 Biotech
Authors: Natasha Maria Barnes, Vishwas B. Khodse, Nikita P. Lotlikar, Ram Murti Meena, Samir R. Damare
Journal: 3 Biotech  |  Year: 2018 (published December 2017)
DOI: 10.1007/s13205-017-1043-8
Problem

Marine fungi from oil-affected niches (mangrove sediments, offshore sediments, tarballs) are a largely untapped reservoir of hydrocarbon-degrading organisms. Screening these ecologically relevant isolates could yield candidates better adapted to saltwater bioremediation than soil-derived strains.

Methods

342 marine fungal isolates obtained from Divar mangrove sediments, Arabian Sea offshore sediments, and tarballs from Betul Beach (Goa, India); 51 fast-growing isolates pre-screened on mineral salt medium with petrol as sole carbon source; 10 isolates with clear growth selected for detailed crude oil degradation assays (23-day incubation); GC-FID for n-alkane profiles; Artemia salina lethality assay for metabolite toxicity.

Key Findings

Ten active isolates comprised 6 Aspergillus spp., 2 Fusarium spp., 1 Penicillium sp., and 1 Acremonium sp. Penicillium citrinum (NIOSN-M126) was the top performer: 77% total crude oil reduction and 95.4% removal of individual n-alkanes (C14–C30). All isolates preferentially degraded long-chain over short-chain alkanes. Metabolites produced were non-toxic per A. salina assay.

Takeaways

Marine mangrove sediments are productive screening environments yielding salt-adapted fungi with high alkane degradation efficiency and safe metabolite profiles, supporting their use in coastal oil spill bioaugmentation.

Limitations

Single 23-day incubation period; no field-scale or mesocosm testing; limited to Indian coastal sites; aromatic fraction degradation not quantified.

Spini et al. (2018) — Molecular and microbiological insights on enrichment procedures for isolation of petroleum-degrading bacteria and fungi Frontiers in Microbiology
Authors: Giulia Spini, Federica Spina, Anna Poli, Anne-Laure Blieux, Tiffanie Regnier, Carla Gramellini, Giovanna C. Varese, Edoardo Puglisi
Journal: Frontiers in Microbiology  |  Year: 2018
DOI: 10.3389/fmicb.2018.02543
Problem

Enrichment culture is routinely used to isolate petroleum-degrading microorganisms, but it is unclear how the choice of hydrocarbon substrate (crude oil vs. liquid paraffin) and sequential enrichment cycles shape the resulting community — and whether culture-dependent isolation faithfully reflects molecular community profiles.

Methods

Contaminated agricultural soil (Italy) subjected to 3 sequential enrichment cycles using either crude oil or liquid paraffin as sole carbon sources; 16S rRNA and ITS2 amplicon sequencing (Illumina) for community profiling at each cycle; culture-dependent isolation yielding 95 bacterial and 94 fungal strains; morphological and ITS-based identification.

Key Findings

Substrate type (paraffin vs. oil) created distinct bacterial communities but did not significantly differentiate fungal communities. Neither depth nor enrichment cycle number significantly drove final community composition. Fusarium was the most abundant fungal genus (94 isolates), followed by Trichoderma and Aspergillus. Culture-dependent and molecular analyses agreed well on dominant genera. 95 bacterial and 94 fungal strains represent a curated bioremediation resource.

Takeaways

Substrate chemistry shapes bacterial but not fungal enrichment trajectories, implying fungi are more metabolically flexible in utilizing varied hydrocarbon fractions. The strong agreement between culture and molecular methods validates traditional isolation for this application.

Limitations

Single contaminated soil site; degradation rates of isolated strains not individually quantified; community dynamics within enrichment cycles not resolved at strain level; field performance of isolates not tested.

Al-Hawash et al. (2019) — Removal and biodegradation of different petroleum hydrocarbons using the filamentous fungus Aspergillus sp. RFC-1 MicrobiologyOpen
Authors: Adnan B. Al-Hawash, Xiaoyu Zhang, Fuying Ma
Journal: MicrobiologyOpen  |  Year: 2019
DOI: 10.1002/mbo3.619
Problem

While Aspergillus spp. are known petroleum degraders, the relative contributions of adsorption, absorption, and active biodegradation to overall hydrocarbon removal are rarely disaggregated. Quantifying these mechanisms is essential for understanding and optimizing fungal bioremediation efficiency.

Methods

Enrichment on mineral salt medium + 1% crude oil (Rumaila oil field, Basra, Iraq); morphological and ITS sequencing for identification; degradation assays with crude oil, naphthalene (NAP), phenanthrene (PHE), and pyrene (PYR); GC-MS for compound quantification; assays distinguishing intracellular enzymes, extracellular enzymes, adsorption, and bioaccumulation contributions.

Key Findings

RFC-1 achieved 7-day degradation of: crude oil 51.8%, NAP 84.6%, PHE 50.3%, PYR 55.1%. Total removal efficiencies (including bioaccumulation) were higher: crude oil 60.3%, NAP 97.4%, PHE 84.9%, PYR 90.7%. PAHs showed substantially greater bioaccumulation than crude oil fractions. Both intracellular and extracellular enzyme systems contributed, with extracellular enzymes playing a larger role in PAH oxidation.

Takeaways

Disaggregating removal (adsorption + bioaccumulation) from true biodegradation is essential: high removal rates may mask limited mineralization. For PAHs, bioaccumulation significantly inflates apparent degradation metrics.

Limitations

Laboratory-scale aqueous conditions; single strain; controlled temperature and nutrients; no soil matrix tested; long-term fate of bioaccumulated PAHs within fungal cells not addressed.

Asemoloye et al. (2020) — Hydrocarbon degradation and enzyme activities of Aspergillus oryzae and Mucor irregularis from Nigerian crude oil-polluted sites Microorganisms (MDPI)
Authors: Asemoloye M.D., Tosi S., Daccò C., Wang X., Xu S., Marchisio M.A., Gao W., Jonathan S.G., Pecoraro L.
Journal: Microorganisms  |  Year: 2020
DOI: 10.3390/microorganisms8121912
Problem

Crude oil pollution in Nigeria's Niger Delta causes severe ecological damage. Identification of indigenous fungi from these highly contaminated sites — adapted to local soil chemistry and climate — is needed for designing effective site-specific bioremediation strategies.

Methods

Soil sampling at 5–20 cm depth from two crude oil-polluted Niger Delta sites (Yorla-10 and Effurun, Nigeria); isolation on PDA; 14 fungal strains identified; dominant strains selected by incidence and morphology; ITS-based molecular identification; Bushnell-Haas medium tolerance testing at 5–20% engine oil; GC/MS for hydrocarbon profile analysis after 15 days; enzyme assays for laccase (Lac), manganese peroxidase (MnP), and lignin peroxidase (LiP).

Key Findings

Mucor irregularis (66.7% incidence) and A. oryzae (56.7% incidence) were dominant. Both tolerated 20% engine oil. A. oryzae degraded a broad spectrum of alkanes and aromatics; M. irregularis targeted long-chain alkanes and BTEX compounds. Both produced Lac > MnP > LiP. GC/MS confirmed removal of C10–C35 alkane fractions.

Takeaways

The dominance of these two genera in heavily polluted Niger Delta soil reflects their ecological fitness under chronic hydrocarbon stress. Their ligninolytic enzyme profiles (Lac-dominant) provide mechanistic insight into aromatic ring cleavage strategies distinct from those of white-rot basidiomycetes.

Limitations

15-day incubation; lab-scale only; gene expression mechanisms not investigated; synergistic effects between co-occurring soil microbiota not assessed.

Cross-Paper Observations

  • DCPIP and mineral medium growth are the universal first-pass screen: Across all studies, initial screening relied on either the colorimetric DCPIP (2,6-dichlorophenolindophenol) reduction assay or growth on mineral salt medium with a hydrocarbon as sole carbon source. These rapid, low-cost methods are the community standard, but they report utilization capacity, not mineralization rate.
  • Aspergillus, Fusarium, and Penicillium dominate isolate libraries: All screening studies recovered these three genera as the most common hydrocarbon-competent isolates, regardless of geography (India, Italy, Iraq, Nigeria, Tunisia). This phylogenetic convergence suggests broad-spectrum hydrocarbon tolerance is ancestral or easily acquired in these lineages.
  • Alkane degradation far outpaces aromatic degradation: Studies measuring both fractions (Gargouri 2015, Barnes 2018, Al-Hawash 2019) consistently found superior degradation of n-alkanes (C11–C30) versus PAHs or BTEX. This reflects the relative recalcitrance of fused-ring aromatics and highlights a persistent functional gap in fungal bioremediation screening programs.
  • Removal ≠ biodegradation — bioaccumulation inflates metrics: Al-Hawash (2019) explicitly demonstrated that PAH "removal" measurements overestimate true biodegradation because a substantial fraction is bioaccumulated within fungal biomass rather than mineralized. Earlier studies that report only total removal (e.g., Gargouri 2015 via gravimetry) may similarly overstate actual degradation.
  • Enrichment substrate shapes bacterial but not fungal communities: Spini et al. (2018) found that fungi enriched similarly on crude oil vs. paraffin, while bacterial communities diverged. This implies fungal enzyme systems are less substrate-specific — an advantage for treating mixed-contamination spills, but a challenge for selecting for particular degradation activities.
  • Open question — ecotoxicological validation is rare: Only Barnes et al. (2018) tested metabolite toxicity (Artemia salina assay). The fate and toxicity of fungal transformation products — including potentially more toxic epoxide or quinone intermediates — remain uncharacterized in most screening studies.
