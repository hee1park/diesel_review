Exploring Novel Fungal-Bacterial Consortia for Enhanced Petroleum Hydrocarbon Degradation
Monteiro et al.  ·  Toxics 2024  ·  Consortium PAH
Problem
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) from petroleum contamination are persistent in soil; cost-effective bioremediation strategies using native microorganisms are needed.
Methods
Isolation of native fungi (n=7) and bacteria (n=12) from diesel/biodiesel-contaminated Brazilian soil (20% biodiesel blend); molecular identification by ITS and 16S sequencing; biosurfactant production assay; laccase activity measurement; four microbial consortia screened for PAH degradation capacity in liquid culture.
Findings
A three-member consortium of Trichoderma koningiopsis, Serratia marcescens, and Burkholderia cepacia achieved ≥91% degradation for all 11 PAHs tested, removing 93.61% of total PAHs — significantly outperforming individual strains. First report of T. koningiopsis for petroleum hydrocarbon bioremediation.
Takeaways
Mixed fungal-bacterial consortia are superior to pure cultures, likely due to complementary enzymatic repertoires and metabolic cross-feeding. Screening from contaminated site microbiomes yields well-adapted degraders.
Limitations
Controlled laboratory conditions only; specific consortium combinations tested without systematic factorial design; no field or mesocosm validation.
Petroleum-Degrading Fungal Isolates for the Treatment of Soil Microcosms
Daâssi & Almaghrabi  ·  Microorganisms 2023  ·  Bioaugmentation TPH
Problem
Total petroleum hydrocarbon (TPH) contamination in soil requires efficient fungal-based bioaugmentation strategies that surpass natural attenuation.
Methods
Screening of native soil fungi plus ligninolytic strains; batch microcosm reactors under aerobic conditions; mono- and co-culture bioaugmentation; TPH quantification after 60-day incubation.
Findings
Paecilomyces formosus achieved the highest TPH degradation (97 ± 2.54%) after 60 days, outperforming Aspergillus niger (92%) and the fungal consortium (84%). Six isolates total demonstrated significant degradation potential.
Takeaways
High-performing single isolates can exceed consortium performance in some systems; Paecilomyces emerges as an underutilized genus for TPH remediation. Aerobic microcosms effectively model soil bioaugmentation.
Limitations
Controlled laboratory microcosms may not replicate field-scale heterogeneity, fluctuating moisture, or competing native microbiota; long-term fungal persistence unverified.
Indigenous Fungi for Hydrocarbon Biodegradation in Diesel-Contaminated Soil
Blanc et al.  ·  Environmental Pollution 2024  ·  Diesel Trichoderma
Problem
Diesel hydrocarbon contamination in soil creates significant environmental and human health hazards; simple, reproducible methods to identify effective indigenous fungal degraders are lacking.
Methods
Isolation of filamentous fungi from contaminated sites in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil; growth screens on diesel-containing media for seven species; morphological and molecular identification; microdilution tests; TPH quantification at multiple time points (7, 14, 16, 30 days).
Findings
Trichoderma asperellum showed superior growth at high diesel concentrations, degrading 41–54% TPH at 10 g/kg diesel within 7–14 days, and 48% at a very high concentration of 1000 g/kg after 30 days. Penicillium pedernalense was the second candidate.
Takeaways
A simple screening methodology using diesel-supplemented agar effectively identifies robust indigenous degraders. T. asperellum tolerates remarkably high hydrocarbon concentrations, making it suitable for heavily polluted sites.
Limitations
Artificially contaminated soil in laboratory; no field validation; intermediate metabolite identification and toxicity assessment not performed.
Anaerobic PAH Biodegradation by Deep-Seafloor Fungi (2.5 km Depth)
Ul Arifeen et al.  ·  Chemosphere 2022  ·  Anaerobic PAH Deep biosphere
Problem
It was unknown whether fungi in deep sub-seafloor anaerobic environments are capable of degrading PAHs and utilizing coal compounds, as all prior studies assumed aerobic conditions as a prerequisite.
Methods
Isolation of fungi from coal-associated sediments 1.3–2.5 km below the seafloor; anaerobic incubation experiments with phenanthrene, pyrene, and benzo[a]pyrene as sole carbon sources over 10–20 days; enzyme activity profiling (carboxylases vs. lignin-modifying enzymes).
Findings
The white-rot fungus Schizophyllum commune 20R-7-F01 degraded 25%, 18%, and 13% of phenanthrene, pyrene, and benzo[a]pyrene respectively under strict anaerobic conditions. Carboxylases — not the classical ligninolytic enzymes — drove degradation, representing a mechanistic departure from aerobic PAH catabolism.
Takeaways
Fungi extend the known boundary of PAH degradation to deep anaerobic ecosystems via carboxylase-mediated pathways. This opens new avenues for bioremediation in oxygen-limited environments such as sediment caps or saturated subsoils.
Limitations
Degradation rates are modest compared to aerobic studies; mechanism pathway products not fully characterized; applicability to surface sediment remediation undemonstrated.
PAH Degradation by Bacterial-Fungal Co-Cultures: Interaction Patterns
Pozdnyakova et al.  ·  Front Biosci (Elite Ed) 2023  ·  Consortium Enzyme
Problem
The interaction dynamics between bacteria and fungi in co-cultures degrading PAH mixtures are poorly understood, including whether synergy, competition, or inhibition dominate.
Methods
Cultivation of bacteria (Azospirillum brasilense) and fungi (Fusarium oxysporum, Talaromyces sayulitensis, Trichoderma viride) in PAH-containing nutrient media; HPLC for degradation quantification; spectrophotometric enzyme assays for oxidases and peroxidases.
Findings
Three interaction patterns were identified: inhibition, partial suppression with enhanced degradation, and full synergy. Co-cultures degraded PAH mixtures more effectively than monocultures overall. First report of T. sayulitensis producing quinone metabolites alongside oxidase/peroxidase activity during PAH catabolism.
Takeaways
Co-culture outcome is partner-specific and cannot be predicted from monoculture data alone; pairing selection is critical. Novel metabolite production by T. sayulitensis suggests underexplored ascomycete enzymatic diversity.
Limitations
Model liquid culture systems differ substantially from soil; only one bacterial species tested; metabolic pathway reconstruction incomplete.
PAH & Petroleum Hydrocarbon Biodegradation by Indigenous Fungi from Iranian Crude-Oil Soil
Fallahi et al.  ·  Scientific Reports 2023  ·  Isolation GC-MS Alkanes
Problem
Oil-spill contamination with PAHs and petroleum hydrocarbons in Iran's Yadavaran oil field causes persistent ecosystem disruption; diverse indigenous fungal degraders remain uncharacterized.
Methods
265 fungal specimens isolated from contaminated field soil; morphological and molecular ITS identification; DCPIP colorimetric screening for hydrocarbon-utilization; GC-MS quantification over 47-day liquid culture and 60-day soil assays.
Findings
Alternaria tenuissima (5c-12) and Epicoccum nigrum (3b-1) were top performers: >70% degradation of long-chain alkanes; 93–99% degradation of high-MW PAHs including pyrene and benzo[a]pyrene. These genera had not previously been highlighted for petroleum bioremediation.
Takeaways
DCPIP colorimetric screening is an efficient high-throughput tool for narrowing large fungal collections to candidate degraders. Uncommon genera (Alternaria, Epicoccum) may harbor significant but overlooked bioremediation potential.
Limitations
Soil assays validated for top-2 isolates only; metabolite identification limited; no assessment of ecotoxicity or fungal establishment in native soil microbiome.
Scale-Up of Mycoremediation for Aliphatic Hydrocarbons: Key Determining Factors
Antón-Herrero et al.  ·  Journal of Fungi 2023  ·  Review Scale-up Aliphatics
Problem
Despite promising laboratory results, mycoremediation rarely translates successfully to pilot or field scales; the factors governing this lab-to-field gap for aliphatic petroleum hydrocarbons are poorly synthesized.
Methods
Comprehensive narrative review synthesizing mycoremediation research on aliphatic hydrocarbons; analysis of bulking agents, fungal inoculum sources (including spent mushroom substrate), contaminant bioavailability, microbial community dynamics, and environmental conditions.
Findings
Critical scale-up factors include: (1) bioavailability enhancement via bulking agents; (2) ligninolytic fungal species selection; (3) exploitation of "fungal highways" enabling bacterial dispersal through soil; (4) synergistic fungal-bacterial community assembly. Spent mushroom substrate is a cost-effective, field-ready inoculum.
Takeaways
Scale-up success depends on ecological engineering — pairing fungal substrate, soil structure, and microbial community simultaneously rather than optimizing strains in isolation. The fungal highway concept re-frames fungi as ecosystem engineers rather than sole degraders.
Limitations
Review-based; limited field-scale empirical data available; performance under varying climatic conditions and soil chemistries underrepresented in source literature.
Mycoremediation Using Native Ganoderma & Trametes from the Ecuadorian Amazon
Cipriani-Avila et al.  ·  Journal of Fungi 2025  ·  White-rot fungi 96% TPH removal
Problem
Oil extraction activities in Ecuador's Amazon have caused widespread soil contamination; conventional remediation techniques are resource-intensive and ecologically damaging, motivating search for native fungal alternatives.
Methods
Isolation and screening of 16 fungal strains from Yasuní National Park; enzymatic activity assessed via plate-based assays and quantitative laccase measurement; top five strains tested in 60-day soil microcosm experiment under controlled conditions.
Findings
All five selected strains — Ganoderma cf. parvulum, three Trametes menziesii isolates, and Trametes meyenii — removed >96% of total petroleum hydrocarbons, versus only 12–24% in uninoculated controls. Laccase activity correlated with degradation efficiency.
Takeaways
Tropical Amazon white-rot fungi from petroleum-contaminated sites are pre-adapted degraders with exceptionally high TPH removal rates. Native strain selection from the target biome is a powerful strategy for bioremediation efficacy.
Limitations
Preliminary laboratory microcosm study; field performance under actual Amazonian conditions (temperature fluctuation, rainfall, soil heterogeneity) remains to be validated.

Cross-Paper Observations

Methodological Patterns

  • All studies use ITS amplicon sequencing for fungal identification — now the universal standard.
  • GC-MS / HPLC are the analytical backbone for quantifying degradation of specific compounds.
  • DCPIP colorimetric assay (Fallahi 2023) is gaining traction as a rapid high-throughput screen for large isolate collections.
  • Soil microcosm duration ranges from 14 to 60 days across studies.

Consensus Findings

  • Fungal-bacterial consortia consistently outperform single-organism cultures (Monteiro 2024, Pozdnyakova 2023).
  • Native isolates from contaminated sites outperform lab strains (Blanc 2024, Cipriani-Avila 2025, Fallahi 2023).
  • White-rot basidiomycetes (Trametes, Ganoderma, Pleurotus) are the most consistently high-performing genera for TPH and PAH degradation.
  • Laccase activity is the primary enzymatic marker for degradation potential screening.

Active Debates

  • Monoculture vs. consortium: Daâssi 2023 finds a single isolate (Paecilomyces) outperforming consortia, while Monteiro 2024 and Pozdnyakova 2023 find the reverse — outcome appears system-dependent.
  • Aerobic vs. anaerobic enzymatic routes: Arifeen 2022 demonstrates carboxylase-driven anaerobic PAH degradation, challenging the laccase/MnP/LiP paradigm dominant in aerobic studies.
  • Lab-to-field extrapolation: Antón-Herrero 2023 highlights that lab efficiency rarely predicts field performance, yet most empirical papers still operate at microcosm scale.

Open Questions

  • Complete metabolic pathway mapping for ascomycete degraders (e.g., Alternaria, Epicoccum, Talaromyces).
  • Ecological fate of fungal inoculants after bioremediation: persistence, dispersal, and impact on native microbiome.
  • Optimization of fungal highway-mediated bacterial dispersal in real soil architecture.
  • Performance under combined stressors (co-contamination with heavy metals, salinity, temperature extremes).
  • Regulatory and cost-feasibility pathways for field-scale mycoremediation deployment.

Enzymatic Mechanisms

  • Basidiomycetes (aerobic): Laccase, Lignin Peroxidase (LiP), Manganese Peroxidase (MnP) — one-electron oxidation of PAHs to quinones.
  • Ascomycetes (aerobic): Cytochrome P450 monooxygenase + epoxide hydrolase — hydroxylation pathway producing arene oxides.
  • Deep-sea fungi (anaerobic): Carboxylase-mediated oxidation — a mechanistically distinct route (Arifeen 2022).
  • Biosurfactant production (Monteiro 2024) enhances bioavailability of hydrophobic PAHs.

Key Emerging Genera

  • Trichoderma asperellum & T. koningiopsis — first reports in petroleum remediation contexts.
  • Alternaria tenuissima and Epicoccum nigrum — surprising high-MW PAH degraders from Iranian oil fields.
  • Talaromyces sayulitensis — novel quinone metabolite producer.
  • Paecilomyces formosus — top TPH remediator, underexplored in practice.
  • Schizophyllum commune — validated for anaerobic deep-seafloor degradation.
